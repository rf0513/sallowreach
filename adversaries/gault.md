# Gault

**Type:** Mire Poacher / Deep-Channel Extractor
**Location:** [[The Mire]] — no fixed base; operates out of a flat-bottomed skiff he keeps cached in different locations along the Wending
**Tier:** 2 (Solo)

*Nobody knows where he's from. He's been in the Mire long enough that it doesn't matter anymore.*

---

## The Anti-Remy

[[Remy Duval]] reads the Mire like a conversation — something alive, something worth paying attention to, something that gives back if you approach it right. Gault reads it like a ledger. Same channels, same creatures, same black water. Completely different relationship with all of it.

He knows the Mire. He knows it extremely well — the channels, the nesting grounds, the specific pools where bioluminescent concentrations are highest, the deep-water territories where the specimens get strange and extraordinary and valuable. He has navigated the Wending hundreds of times without a chart. He has been in places Remy has been in twice and Remy has been in the Mire for twenty years.

The difference is what he does when he gets there. He extracts. He harvests. He loads what he can carry into waterproofed containers and sells it to whoever is paying: the Alchemists' Consortium, imperial research facilities, private collectors who want something that glows on their shelf. He leaves damaged nesting grounds and stripped pools and disturbed territories behind him, and he does not look back, and he does not think about it.

He has been doing this for eight years. He will do it until something in the Mire finally kills him, which has not happened yet despite several reasonable attempts.

---

## Appearance

Lean in the specific way of someone whose body has resolved all unnecessary weight. Sun-dark skin, hands that are scarred in a dozen places from a dozen different creatures, and the particular stillness of someone who has learned to move through environments that punish noise. He dresses in Mire-practical layers — waterproofed, dark, nothing that catches.

**His eyes are wrong.** Not dramatically wrong — subtly wrong. In normal light, nothing you could name. In the dark, in the Mire, when the bioluminescence is active: they pick up the light and hold it a half-second too long. Like something behind the iris is retaining it. He has been in the spirit-charged water of the deep channels for years, wading through what tethered the party in minutes. Whatever the water does to people who enter it without reverence — Gault is eight years deep in that experiment. He doesn't glow. He doesn't hear voices. He just has eyes that do something wrong in the dark, and a sense of direction in the Mire that operates on a frequency Remy cannot entirely explain.

He carries: a short harpoon, a set of weighted nets in various sizes, a row of sealed containers strapped across his back, and a long knife he has used on things other than Mire creatures exactly twice. He is thinking about a third time.

---

## Personality

Contemptuous of everything that costs him time and pays him nothing. Remy's relationship with the Mire — the names, the reverence, the *"isn't she magnificent"* — he finds genuinely, specifically stupid. Not threatening. Stupid. A man who could be extracting resources is instead making friends with them.

He is not dramatic about his contempt. He states it plainly, once, and moves on. He does not argue. He does not explain himself. He has found that people who want to argue about the ethics of Mire extraction tend to be standing between him and his skiff, and that is a solvable problem.

**He is smug when he escapes.** Not loudly — a particular quality of unhurried departure that communicates *I was never worried.* He has escaped bad situations in the Mire enough times that he has stopped treating them as bad situations. The party will find this infuriating. They are supposed to find this infuriating.

**The specific line that stays with the party:**

First encounter. Remy has just discovered the brightfin basin has been worked — nets, signs of extraction, several individuals taken. Remy goes very still in the way that means serious. Gault is still there, loading the last container. He looks at Remy's expression and says:

*"Name them if you want. Makes the inventory easier to track."*

He means it practically. That is the worst part.

## The Mire-Touched Quality

Eight years of deep-channel work has done something to him. He doesn't talk about it because he doesn't think about it. It's just what the work does.

**Mire predators don't attack him.** Not because they respect him — because he reads as wrong to them. Something about his scent, his bioluminescent signature, the way the spirit-water has altered his chemistry over years of immersion. Animals avoid carrion. They avoid Gault the same way. He has used this practically: he goes places in the Mire that would be dangerous for anyone else, and things leave him alone. He does not consider what this means about what he has become.

**He doesn't get lost.** Not the way Remy doesn't get lost — Remy knows the Mire. Gault is followed by something in the Mire. Some thread of attention, impersonal and cold, that keeps him oriented. He navigates by it without knowing he's navigating by it. If asked how he finds his way in the deep channels he says *"experience"* and means it, and is only half wrong.

What is actually tracking him: unknown. [GM: consider whether this is the Mangrove Spirit's peripheral awareness of something that keeps disturbing its territory, or something older and more local. Leave it unnamed.]

## Operations

He works alone, or with hired hands he picks up in port towns outside Sallowreach and drops off after each run. The hired hands know the job is extraction and don't ask about permits because the pay is good and Gault doesn't answer questions. He has gone through eleven hired hands in eight years. Some left. Some didn't make it out of the Mire. He has no feelings about this.

His buyers are not in Sallowreach. He sells through intermediaries in port cities — no paper trail back to him, no names attached to the goods. By the time a collector in the capital puts a glowing creature on their shelf, Gault is three runs and four hundred miles of Mire removed from the transaction.

He is aware of [[Remy Duval]] in the way that two people who operate in the same territory become aware of each other. He finds Remy's monopoly on legitimate Mire access professionally irritating and personally irrelevant. He does not have legitimate access. He does not need it. The Mire does not check permits.

## The Three Encounters

*GM structure. Run these across Acts 1–2. Full encounter details — including the Tessak, the lantern rays, and the ecological stakes — live in [[Lantern Rays]]. This section is the Gault-specific view of each encounter.*

### Encounter 1 — Dusk on the Wending *(Early Act 2)*

The party sees the lantern rays for the first time — properly, in safety, with Remy narrating in barely-contained reverence. Then they find the nets. Then Gault.

He is still loading the last container. He does not startle. He looks at them with the specific assessment of someone calculating exit options and finding them favorable.

Remy objects. Gault says the line: *"Name them if you want. Makes the inventory easier to track."*

He has two hired hands covering his exit on the far bank. He withdraws with what he has, unhurried. He looks back once.

What he's taken: lantern rays in sealed containers. Their bioluminescent organs are what he's after — the oil inside is worth more per pound than anything else he's ever extracted. The rays will die in the containers within days. He knows this. The buyer doesn't require them alive.

**The specific cruelty:** *"They stop glowing after a few days in the jar. Doesn't matter. Collectors aren't buying the glow. They're buying the provenance."*

### Encounter 2 — The Tessak Camp *(Mid-Act 2)*

Following the extraction damage southwest, the party finds Gault's staging operation — and the [[Lantern Rays#The Tessak|Tessak]], the lizardfolk clan he's recruited. Gault may or may not be present; he moves constantly.

This encounter is primarily about the Tessak and the possibility of separating them from Gault's operation. See [[Lantern Rays]] for the full Tessak situation, Drevath, and the resolution path.

Gault's role here: if present, he watches the party's conversation with Drevath with the specific attention of someone assessing whether his operation is about to be disrupted. He does not intervene in the negotiation — he doesn't need to, he thinks. He's wrong about that, if the party plays it right.

If the party successfully separates the Tessak from Gault: he loses his workforce. He continues alone. He's stubborn and greedy and the concentrations are getting stronger the further southwest he goes, and he is not going to stop.

### Encounter 3 — The Deep Mire *(Act 2–3)*

The party is following the [[Fishing Spear]] toward the [[Mangrove Spirit]]. Gault has been following the same bioluminescent concentrations, thinking he's tracking a massive undiscovered population of lantern rays. He's wrong about what's producing the light. He is in entirely the wrong place for entirely the wrong reasons.

He is alone. He has no exit the party doesn't know about. He is, for the first time, slightly out of his depth — and too greedy to acknowledge it.

The fight happens in spirit-adjacent territory, in bioluminescent dark that has nothing to do with lantern rays. The water is lit from something vast and old. Something is paying attention — not to the fight, just to the territory, in the ambient way the Mangrove Spirit is always present in the deep channels.

After he's dead: the Mire goes still. The bioluminescence disperses slowly. No one mentions it. No one needs to.

**What this does:** The party has been waiting for this. Two escapes, two smug departures, a school that's smaller than it should be, children sick in the outer Mire because a man needed a workforce. This is the fight they earned. No regrets.

## Key Relationships

- [[Remy Duval]] — professional contempt that is entirely one-directional; Remy finds him an active offense against the Mire; Gault finds Remy sentimental and irrelevant; they have had three confrontations in eight years, each time Remy unable to do anything; this will be the fourth
- [[The Mire]] — his warehouse; has no other relationship with it; the Mire has changed him anyway, indifferent to his indifference

## Dialogue Hooks

**On Remy's relationship with the Mire:**
*"Name them if you want. Makes the inventory easier to track."*

**On what he takes:**
*"They stop glowing after a week or so in the jar. Doesn't matter. Collectors aren't buying the glow, they're buying the provenance."*

**On being confronted about permits or damage:**
*"The Mire was here before your city and it'll be here after. It doesn't need your protection."*

**On the party specifically, after the first escape:**
*"You're in the Mire looking for something. Everyone who comes this deep is looking for something."* [looks at them with those slightly-wrong eyes] *"You won't find it before I'm done here."*

**If somehow cornered early:**
*"You're not the worst thing I've dealt with in here."* [and he means it, which is unsettling]
