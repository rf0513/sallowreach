# Isotta — Arc: The Uncollected

> **Status: ACTIVE.** This replaces the investigator plan in [[isotta-arc-brainstorm]] (now superseded — salvage the salt-wall set piece, discard the rest). Tuned for what her player actually lights up for: **mystery and wonder**, with ghost-pathos as seasoning and horror kept to *eerie*.

## The Answer (GM only — the whole arc in one paragraph)

Death is a two-step transaction: the body stops, then the ferryman collects the soul and ties the knot. Isotta's step one happened on the ritual table. Mid-collection, the transformation **yanked her soul back and tied it to the living side** — step two never completed, and her line in **[[the-tallykeeper|the Tallykeeper]]**'s ledger has hung open ever since. This is the single explanation behind every symptom: her edges fray, the dark pulls, the veil drags — the world keeps trying to *finish the transaction*.

**In plain table language: she cannot die — but she can cease.** Her death already happened; it can't happen twice. So instead of dying, she *frays* — and if she frays to nothing, there is nothing left to collect: no crossing, no other side, just erasure, and a line that stays open forever. "Becoming whole" doesn't mean getting her life back for free; it means becoming **properly mortal again** — someone who can, someday, truly die, and be collected, and cross.

**Why Sallowreach cuts both ways:** the thin veil is where her cure is — and the only place the Tallykeeper can finally reach her. There has been a heron on the roofline every night since she arrived. Her *Animal Whisperer* experience is how she notices.

## The Case She's Already Carrying

**Do not tell her any of the above. Let her detect it.** Her player loves investigation, and her sheet already gives her the perfect vehicle: the casebook, written in the Order's flat observational prose. So make her condition the case — the file labeled, in her own clinical hand, *Subject: myself.*

Every symptom below is **evidence**, and the pattern is solvable. The Order taught her exactly how to work a haunting: document manifestations, identify the anchor, determine what the spirit is owed. She has simply never run the protocol on the one ghost she lives with. When she finally does — when she lays her own symptoms out like a case wall — the deduction is *right there*: this is not the profile of a cursed soul. **This is the profile of an unpaid debt.**

This fixes the old plan's core flaw. She's not investigating the campaign's lore. She's investigating *herself* — and the campaign keeps handing her comparative evidence (every docket case below is a data point about how death is *supposed* to work, which is how she notices hers didn't).

## The Condition — Costs & Gifts

*(All homebrew — pitch to the player before play, adjust freely. `[VERIFY]` each against the SRD's Stress/countdown rules before the table.)*

### Costs
- **Her own toolkit bites her.** Salt stings her palms; her warding circles won't admit her; her rites work on *her*. Every hunt-prep scene, she's handling weapons pointed at herself.
- **Taste is leaving her — except for meaning.** Food is ash unless it *carries* something: a meal cooked with love, a drink tied to a memory, bread from a grieving hand. Ghosts feed on significance, not substance. ([[Vael Miroux]] quietly figures this out and starts serving her accordingly at [[The Still Cup]], and never once says so.)
- **The dark pulls.** (Canon — she nearly came apart the night before Session 3's end.) Concretely: she cannot safely sleep alone in full dark. She needs an anchor — a lit lantern, or a living heartbeat within arm's reach. *Party glue: someone holds the tether, literally.*
- **Her reflection runs half a beat late.** And it looks back from the spirit side. Animals and small children track the reflection, not her.
- **The newly dead find her.** She is a lit doorway in a city where people drown. They come to her window. (This cost is also the engine — see the Lantern Docket.)

### Gifts
- **The water can't kill her.** She doesn't drown. In a drowning city, the half-ghost is the one thing the Mire can't take — and the deep channels, closed to the living, are open to her.
- **Veilstep.** Once per rest she can slip: through a barrier, unseen by the living, briefly to the other side. Costs Stress and ticks the Slipping clock. `[VERIFY — cost/uses vs SRD]`
- **Second sight.** She perceives Veilside Sallowreach — the city's spirit-side overlay, where the lanterns burn a different color (see below).
- **Death has no file on her.** Reflavor her *Avoid Death* move: it isn't luck — death literally cannot find her paperwork. Funny, right up until she deduces *why*. `[VERIFY — death moves per SRD]`

### The Slipping Clock
A 6-segment countdown. The GM may tick it with a Fear on rolls made in darkness or thin-veil places, or when she over-reaches with ghost-side gifts. At full, she begins to come apart — half-crossing, outline guttering — until grounded. What a full clock ultimately threatens is not death — she can't — it's **dissolution**: fraying toward erasure, no crossing, nothing left to collect. (After the introduction, a filled clock also draws one loop of the grace cord tight — see [[the-tallykeeper#The Grace Period]].) **The grounding technique** (Vael teaches it; the salt-wall quest banks it) clears segments: a named anchor, spoken aloud — her own name, a held hand, lantern-light. `[VERIFY — countdown mechanics vs SRD]`

## The Tallykeeper

The heron has its own sheet now: **[[the-tallykeeper]]** — six titles (the countable age-clue, per the naming lore in [[Cosmology]]), both-sides-of-the-veil appearance, parley etiquette, the Old Patience disambiguation, and its GM guardrails all live there. What the arc needs at a glance:

- It is **owed her death** and it has come to **collect it** — not from malice, from arithmetic: if she frays to nothing there is nothing to collect and her line stays open forever, the one entry it cannot make. A late, wrong-shaped collection still balances the ledger. Dissolution never does.
- The bargain is never "life or death, pick one" — it's **time**: a revocable grace period, bought with collateral (closed docket cases), while she races for the cure. Full terms and the grace-cord prop: [[the-tallykeeper#The Grace Period]].
- It **does not speak first** — Session 4 is pure stalking — but it will not refuse a direct question twice. If she insists, the introduction fires early. Never meant to be fought.

## The Reveal — two stages, her favorite shape

1. **Eerie (Act 1–2):** herons, everywhere, patient. The dead she helps whisper about "the Tallykeeper" with reverence, not fear — which reads as fear anyway. Her casebook fills with anomalies that don't fit the Order's taxonomy. Hold the line from her sheet: *she perceives, she does not understand.* No NPC hands her the answer — [[Vael Miroux]] can confirm evidence ("that is not haunting-sign") but genuinely doesn't know what it is; this is past even him.
2. **The Introduction (the flip):** the deduction lands — likely at a docket case where a death is collected *properly* in front of her and she finally sees the transaction she never completed. Then the Tallykeeper introduces itself — in a later session on its own schedule, **or early, any night she directly and insistently addresses it (it will not refuse a direct question twice)** — and the horror inverts into something cleaner and worse: it was never hunting her. It has come to **collect, while there is still enough of her to take** — and the only thing on the table is **time**. The parley is not "live or die"; it is negotiating the grace period ([[the-tallykeeper#The Grace Period]]), with her closed cases as collateral.

## The Lantern Docket — the sidequest engine

Because she's a lit doorway, the dead of Sallowreach queue up. Case-of-the-session mysteries, each one small, each secretly asking her arc question in miniature — *what makes a person whole: the body, the memory, or being known?* — and each one a data point about how crossing is *supposed* to work.

Run them as proper little mysteries (her player's lane): a client with a want, evidence that doesn't add up, a solution that's never "exorcise it."

- **The Drowned Poler.** Won't cross until his boat goes to his daughter — who sold it a week ago to make rent at the Mooring. *Teaches: anchors.* (Wires into [[Remy Duval]]'s boat economy; the party's boat fund suddenly has a rival claim with a dead man's blessing on it.)
- **The Celebration Ghost.** Stuck reliving the best night of her life, fading a little more with each loop. Isotta must judge whether a perfect memory is a haven or a trap. *Teaches: what keeps a spirit from moving. And it's a mirror — a soul refusing to finish its crossing.*
- **The Deathbed.** A dying animal that isn't afraid of her — she is the only comfortable presence at its side, and it is glad she's there. No mystery, no roll. *Teaches: what she could be, if "whole" turns out to mean something no cure sells.* (Her *Animal Whisperer* at full ache.)

**Engine rule:** every case she closes, she is doing the Tallykeeper's job for it — and every closed case draws the heron **one roof closer.** The docket literally feeds the spine. When she notices *that* pattern in her casebook, stage two is ready to fire. And after the introduction, closed cases become her **collateral** — each one loosens a loop of the grace cord ([[the-tallykeeper#The Grace Period]]).

### How a Crossing Works (when she tries to help one over)

She will try — probably immediately, with Anselme. The rules, and what each attempt teaches:

- **She cannot push a ghost across.** He steps toward wherever she points and the anchor snaps him back like a mooring line. *Lesson (rule #1 of her own cure): crossing isn't a door — it's an account that has to balance.* Unfinished business is physics here, not metaphor.
- **The Order's rites are a severing, not an ushering.** If she reaches for her training, the words land on the ghost like the salt line does — her toolkit is a scalpel where she needed a hand to hold. If she completes a rite anyway, the spirit isn't ushered; it's *banished*, torn loose unfinished. (Vael, after: *"The Order teaches you to cut the mooring line, girl. Nobody ever taught us how to bring the boat home."*)
- **Her real power is the bridge.** She stands in both worlds, so she can do what no living medium can: carry the goodbye across — relay the dead to the living they can't reach, hold a hand on each side of a line only she can straddle. Settling the anchor is usually *this*, not a fetch-quest.

**When the account balances, the Tallykeeper comes to collect** — and the first time she witnesses it is a midpoint jewel; stage it with care:

> It comes at low tide, when there's no wind. And the first thing it does is bow. The dead bow back — somehow they all know to. Then it takes the newest cord off its ledger and ties the knot. One motion. Unhurried. Like a fisherman who's tied ten thousand of these. And here's the thing — the crossing isn't a *taking*. It's more like setting something down. Gently. Two hundred years of practice in one small gesture.

She is watching the exact transaction that never completed for her. Casebook entry: *Observed: a correct death. Subject felt: envy.* And the departing dead may leave her a threshold line — the dead see clearly, and this is the obvious-clue channel (Anselme's: *"I seen your cord, miss. No knot in it. Smooth as riverglass — worn from handling. It keeps yours near the top."* Fires Secrets #4 and #10.)

**Closing a case (mechanics):** clear her Slipping clock and grant a Hope `[VERIFY]`; after the introduction, also loosen one loop of the grace cord — settling accounts calms the wound in the order of things around her. So helping the dead is literally how she manages her condition… and every closed case draws the Tallykeeper one roof closer. **She is medicating with the very thing that has come to collect her.**

The salt-wall set piece is fully built as [[session-04-salt-wall]] — the lingering ghost there (**Anselme Thouvenel**, the Drowned Poler above) is her first docket case, not a clue dispenser.

## Veilside Sallowreach — the wonder budget

Her second sight opens the campaign's best exclusive real estate: the spirit-side city. Lanterns a color that has no name on the living side. The dead keep their own version of the Celebration — and eventually, **she is invited.** Play the double edge and let her player decide which it is: the loneliest party in two worlds, or the first place she has ever unambiguously belonged. *(Keep [[spirit-world-metaphysics]] restraint: spirits are people, wonder over horror, the binding stays secret.)*

## Endgame — a menu, not a script

*(Per the no-hard-coded-endings rule: these are live options, chosen at the table.)*

The mid-campaign introduction buys **time**, not resolution. The cure is a separate, final scene — and the division of powers keeps it honest: **the Tallykeeper can only *sever* the ritual's tie, and severing is collection.** Only [[the-tidebreaker|The Tidebreaker]] can *unbind* it properly. The moment the tie comes unbound, she is collectible again for one held breath — and the ferryman will be standing there. It always is. Live options in that breath (per the no-hard-coded-endings rule):

- **Step back into life.** Fully mortal. Someday it comes for her again, properly, at the end of a whole life — and that's the point. Whole means finishable.
- **Out of grace.** If the last loop closed before she got here, the scene inverts: collection is *due*, and the spear becomes leverage for one final renegotiation the player has to actually win. (This is the pressure that makes the grace cord real — don't rescue her from it for free.)
- **Choose the crossing.** She has the right to decide her account closes now — knot tied, gently, on her own terms. If the campaign has done its work, this is a real option, not a loss.
- **The unwritten one** the player invents when they understand the stakes better than we do.

## What This Arc Deliberately Doesn't Do

- **No lore-conduit relapse.** Her mystery is *her*. The great binding stays the party's shared story; the Tallykeeper touches it in exactly one withheld line.
- **No backstory over-authoring.** The famous family, the ritual's culprits — still the player's to own. The arc never waits on them, but if a name emerges, the Tallykeeper was on a roofline that day too, and remembers.
- **No collision with the tether.** Her personal cure can land mid-to-late campaign; the shared tether still holds her to the finale, per her sheet's "taste now, cure at the finale" split.

## Secrets & Clues — the Tallykeeper file

*(Ten, abstract from discovery, per the Lazy DM rules. Feed ~2 per session through docket cases, animal behavior, casebook review, Vael, or the dead themselves.)*

1. There has been a grey heron within sight of Isotta every day since she reached Sallowreach.
2. Mire folk never chase a heron off a sickroom roof, and none of them can say why anymore.
3. The dead speak of "the tallykeeper" with reverence, the way sailors speak of the tide.
4. Every properly-dying thing in the Mire is met by something; the meeting is gentle and takes only a moment.
5. Her symptoms don't match any haunting in the Order's taxonomy — they match a *ledger error*.
6. The salt wall doesn't reject the dead; it rejects the *uncrossed*. There's a difference, and she's on the wrong side of it.
7. A knotted-cord ledger hangs in her dreams; one cord is smooth and unknotted, and she wakes holding her own wrist.
8. The night she died on the table, every heron within a day's flight of the ritual site took wing at once.
9. The Tallykeeper has never once, in two hundred years, flown over the heart of the city.
10. An uncollected death doesn't just wound the one who owes it — something on the other side has been carrying the ache too.
