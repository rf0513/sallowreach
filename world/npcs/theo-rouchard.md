# Théo Rouchard

**Role:** Head of the Woodworkers' Guild; the most powerful civilian in Sallowreach
**Location:** [[Sallowreach]] — the Guild Hall, craftwork district; personal workshop on the same block (rarely used)
**Faction:** Woodworkers' Guild
**Tier:** 2

---

## Appearance

A man in his mid-fifties with broad shoulders gone slightly soft — the build of someone who was in the workshop every day until he wasn't, and has spent the last twenty years at tables instead. His hands are the contradiction: calloused and scarred at the knuckles, the hands of someone who grew up working, but the nails are clean now and he wears a signet ring on his right hand that costs more than most people in the city make in a year. He dresses well without dressing loudly. Silver at the temples, darker in between, and he keeps it neat. He has the easy posture of someone who has never had to make himself smaller in a room.

---

## Personality & Mannerisms

Genuinely warm in the way that costs him nothing, because nothing has ever cost him much. He remembers names. He asks follow-up questions about things you mentioned in passing three weeks ago. He means it.

**He talks about wood the way some people talk about wine** — with real knowledge and real feeling, the language of someone who grew up with a specific material and never stopped paying attention to it. This is the one area where he isn't coasting. He has opinions about grain direction, about southwest planks versus northeast, about what moisture does to density over a decade. He's right about most of them.

**He has opinions generally, and expresses them readily**, because his opinions have never cost him anything. He hasn't learned to be careful with them. He'll tell you what he thinks about the guild's export pricing, the punt network's quarterly fees, the musicians' Accord's conservatism — plainly, without hedging. He will not realize, until much later, that this is a form of privilege.

**He's comfortable with silence.** Doesn't fill it. This reads as confidence. It is also just comfort.

**The tell:** when he's uncomfortable, he talks about wood. Not deflection — retreat to solid ground. If a conversation touches something he'd rather not examine, the grain of a particular plank suddenly becomes very interesting.

He's been a widower for six years. He has not figured out what to do with that, and he doesn't talk about it.

---

## What He Wants

To maintain what he has — not out of greed, but out of genuine satisfaction. The guild runs well. The lumber ships on schedule. The craftspeople under him do exceptional work and are compensated fairly for it. He has built nothing; he has inherited everything; and he has told himself, and mostly believes, that being a good steward of something you didn't build is its own kind of accomplishment.

He is mostly right about this.

---

## What He's Hiding

When Théo was nineteen, he had a daughter with a girl from the outer boardwalks — a punt operator's daughter, someone his family would not have approved of, someone he was not brave enough to fight for. The girl moved on. The child was placed with a good family: the Auberts, outer market district, a carpenter and his wife who'd wanted children and couldn't have them. The arrangement was made quietly through an intermediary. The Aubert family was told the mother couldn't care for the child. They were not told who the father was.

The child — Lise, she goes by Lise — is seven years old. She doesn't know she's adopted. She has her adoptive father's jaw and her mother's eyes and Théo's hands, and she is, by all accounts that reach him through careful listening, a happy child.

Every year on her birthday, Théo arranges an anonymous gift through whatever channels he can manage without his name attaching to it. He has never met her. He watched her once, from across the market, and walked away before he could do something he couldn't take back.

He is deeply ashamed of this — not of the child, never of the child — but of his own cowardice. He was nineteen and he chose comfort over responsibility and the choice calcified and now it is simply the shape of his life, and he doesn't know how to change it without destroying what the Auberts have built.

## The Quest — The Brightfin

Lise will be eight in two weeks. Théo has learned, through the kind of careful listening a man does when he's paying attention to someone he won't speak to, that she loves animals. That she keeps a small garden of Mire plants on her family's boardwalk. That she told her mother she wanted something *"that glowed."*

**The brightfin** is a small amphibious creature native to a specific shallow basin off the southeast Wending — roughly the size of a large hand, somewhere between a salamander and a fish in body plan, with a bioluminescent stripe along their lateral line that pulses blue-green in patterns that seem almost responsive to mood. They are among the Mire's most beloved creatures: gentle, long-lived, and — critically — they thrive as companions. Punt families have kept them for generations. A brightfin in a well-maintained water basin will live twenty years, eat Mire insects and small crustaceans without complaint, and learn to recognize the people who care for it. They are not a wild thing trapped in captivity. They are, by all evidence, happier with the company.

They are also extraordinarily rare and nearly impossible to catch. They don't fight — they simply evade with an unhurried precision that defeats every straightforward approach. The only way to catch one alive and uninjured is patience, the right angle, and ideally someone who knows exactly where they nest. [[Remy Duval]] knows exactly where they nest. He has been waiting for someone to ask him to go there for years.

**Why he needs the party:** He cannot use anyone who knows him. The Aubert family lives six streets from the Guild Hall. If a familiar face delivers a gift and word gets back — and word always gets back, in a city where everyone has known everyone their whole lives — eleven years of careful anonymity collapse. He needs people with no social threads in Sallowreach. People who can take a commission, do it, and not gossip about it to someone who knows someone who knows the family on the outer market.

Remy recommends them. Remy doesn't ask why Théo needs outsiders. This is one of the reasons Théo has always liked Remy.

**How he frames it:** A specimen commission. A brightfin, alive and uninjured, for a client who prefers to remain anonymous. He offers fair pay. He gives them the container.

The container is a hand-carved wooden box with a water basin inlaid — mesh ventilation cut into the lid in a pattern of Mire grass, the joinery cleaner than anything made for purely practical use. Beautiful work. His own work, done at night in the workshop he rarely uses anymore. He does not explain why the container is so fine. He doesn't quite meet their eyes when he hands it over.

**The quest:** Remy takes them to the basin in full Steve Irwin mode. He has the approach worked out in his head — specific angle, specific stillness, a specific patience that is harder than it sounds in a living swamp. The complication is whatever else the basin holds that day. [GM: a territorial creature, the water behaving oddly near the southwest channel, a rival who heard about the nest — keep it fun rather than fatal.]

When they catch one — and if they follow Remy's method, they will — the brightfin is worth the trip. The bioluminescent stripe pulses blue-green. It sits in Remy's hands for a moment before the transfer, looks at them, and the whole punt goes quiet.

*"She's healthy,"* Remy says, to no one in particular. *"This one's going to a good home."* He says this to every brightfin he's ever caught, which is three.

**The delivery:** Théo takes the container. He looks at the brightfin for a long moment.

*"She'll be eight tomorrow."*

He catches himself. Pays them. Thanks them without explaining.

If the party follows out of curiosity — they watch him walk six streets, slip the container onto a boardwalk step, and leave without knocking. He doesn't look back.

**The reveal:** Days later, someone mentions the Aubert girl has been carrying a glowing creature everywhere. Won't put it down. Says it knows her name.

## Campaign Function

**The brightfin quest:** A palate cleanser between heavier scenes — something that shows Sallowreach has private lives, that the city is full of stories the party isn't the center of. Also establishes Théo as a resource and a person worth knowing.

**As things develop:** He starts to matter on the main plot axis. He's noticed the wood changing — subtly, in ways no one else would catch, because he's worked with it his whole life. Southwest planks, specifically. Something in the density, the grain. Nothing's gone wrong yet. He's trying not to worry about it. *"I keep meaning to look into it."* He won't. He doesn't want to find something that requires a decision.

**If the truth reaches him:** The campaign's weight can land on him. Everything he has — the guild, the trade, four generations of family history — is downstream of an imprisoned spirit. If the party looks him in the face and tells him, the most powerful civilian in Sallowreach is suddenly facing a hard choice he's never had to make. He'll want to defer. To wait. To hope it resolves itself.

If the party tries to bring the truth to the city and let Sallowreach choose its own fate (one of the shapes a resolution could take — see [[resolution-options]]), Théo is the civilian most likely to be their way in. If the party has built a real relationship — helped him, been honest with him, not just used him — he may find the courage he's never needed before. The moment will be quiet, not dramatic. He'll be very still, the way he is with wood, and he'll say: *"If I stand up in front of the city and tell them this — it ends the guild. It ends everything my family built."*

And then, if the party has earned it:

*"Tell me what I need to say."*

## Dialogue Hooks

**On the lumber, to anyone who listens:**
*"I can tell you which part of the Mire a board came from by running my hand along the grain. Southwest planks are denser — darker, heavier. My grandfather said it was the water chemistry. I've never had a better explanation."* [beat] *"Lately though — lately the southwest wood's been different. Subtle. I keep meaning to look into it."*

**On the guild:**
*"It runs itself, mostly. Forty years of good practice. You just don't interfere with a thing that works."*

**On the Celebration, warmly:**
*"My family has kept the east feast table for four generations. My grandmother fed six hundred people off that table the year of the flood. That's what this city is. That's what we do."*

**On the brightfin commission, if pressed:**
*"The client values their privacy. So do I."*

**If the party figures it out and approaches him gently:**
Very still. Then: *"She looked like her mother."* He doesn't say anything else for a while.
