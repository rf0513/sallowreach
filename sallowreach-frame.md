# SALLOWREACH

***Strangers wake cursed at the edge of a living swamp — and the bayou town that holds their answers is hiding one it doesn't know it has.***

## THE PITCH

*Read this to your players before session zero.*

> *Your ship didn't make it through the Mire. The black water took you — and whatever is in that water took something in return. You woke on the bank as strangers, bound by magic you don't understand to the Mire and to the bayou town of Sallowreach. You can't leave. Until you figure out what the swamp did to you, every road out ends the same way. Sallowreach is warm, its food is extraordinary, and sixty thousand people didn't ask for you either — but here you all are.*

## OVERVIEW

*Share this with your players before character creation.*

Sallowreach shouldn't exist — or so outsiders say. A city of sixty thousand souls built on stilts above black bayou water, reachable only by hours of punting through shifting channels with no road in or out. No garrison. No noble patron. No strategic position on any map that matters. And yet Sallowreach is, by every measure that counts, one of the wealthiest cities in the region.

The answer is the Mire. The vast, bioluminescent swamp that surrounds Sallowreach produces alchemical ingredients that don't grow anywhere else — mosses, venoms, luminescent algae that healers and mages pay serious money to import. The timber from Mire trees is extraordinarily dense and waterproof, prized by builders who can't explain why it outperforms anything from managed forests. The brackish channels feed sixty thousand people extraordinarily well, and Sallowreach's culinary tradition — generations deep, entirely its own — has made the city famous in ways its lumber and alchemy cannot. The artisans produce work that takes generations to learn. The punt operators control the only transit through the Mire and tax it accordingly.

Almost everyone here was born here. Roughly nineteen in twenty Sallowreachers are multigenerational — families whose grandparents' grandparents built the first boardwalks. About three thousand immigrants have come, survived the Mire's brutal learning curve, and stayed long enough to be considered almost local. And then there are the others: guild representatives, imperial trade attachés, specialized merchants on rotating postings, all of them here because their organizations need a presence in the city and none of them wanting to be. They live in the designated quarters, complain about the humidity, and count the days until rotation. The Mages Guild's posted wizard is one of these.

What keeps Sallowreach from being conquered is structural. The Mire cannot be crossed by an army — it has been tried, and the attempts are taught in Sallowreach schools with considerable pride. Economic capture is equally impossible; the city doesn't need outside supply chains. So the Empire and the guilds send representatives, negotiate carefully, and pay what Sallowreach charges. They know exactly what they are. Most of them find it humiliating. A few, after enough years here, stop finding it humiliating and stop wanting to go home.

Sallowreach does not perform for outsiders. It trades on its own terms and has never had to beg. Visitors are tolerated, sometimes warmly welcomed — but they are always, unmistakably, guests in someone else's city.

Sallowreach is deeply spiritual — not religious, exactly, but practically, bodily spiritual in the way that living above black water in a magical swamp makes you. The city knows it shares the Mire with forces larger than itself. It doesn't claim to know how many, or their names — that would be presumptuous. It calls them **honored guests**: presences deserving of welcome and respect, the way any guest in your home deserves welcome and respect. You don't command an honored guest. You don't make demands. You make them comfortable, signal that they are welcome, and put on the best table you can.

**The Celebration** is the weekly expression of that hospitality — and at sixty thousand people, it is something to witness. Every week, without fail: music before dawn in the outer districts, building through the day until by evening the entire city is in motion. Chanting in old words. Dancing in patterns that vary by family and district, passed down through generations. Feasting at communal tables that stretch the length of major thoroughfares. Canal-streets full of decorated punt boats carrying musicians. Light-lanterns released over the black water, drifting toward the Mire — a gesture of welcome, an open door. Different neighborhoods have their own traditions within the larger whole. Missing it feels wrong in your bones, even after only a week in the city.

**The Mire is growing.** Slowly, but measurably. Boardwalks that were clear two seasons ago are choked with roots. Fishing channels are narrowing. The city's outer edges are being swallowed inch by inch. The [[Mages Guild]] maintains a salt-ward at Sallowreach's perimeter — managed by their appointed wizard — that holds the growth back, but only just. It's holding. For now.

The swamp has always had spirit presence. The old stories call it the [[Mangrove Spirit]] — vast, ancient, not malicious exactly, but utterly indifferent to human scale. Locals leave offerings at certain roots and avoid certain channels after dark, not from fear exactly, but from the same instinct that keeps you quiet in a cathedral. Something is here. Something notices, in its way.

Your ship didn't make it through the Mire. The black water is magically charged — whatever that means, whatever put it there, it does something to people who go under in it. You went under. You woke on the bank beside strangers you've never met, and you are now, somehow, bound to them. Bound to this place.

The Mire will let you go deep into its channels, through the Wending, into territory even locals avoid — but it will not let you leave it entirely. Every road out of the swamp ends the same way: resistance, then agony, then you're back where you started. You've tried. Everyone tries.

You don't know each other. You didn't choose each other. Until you figure out what the black water did to you and how to undo it, you are stuck in the same place.

#### Wanderborne [VERIFY: exact name in SRD]

Travelers and people without fixed roots are a familiar sight in Sallowreach — or rather, familiar in that they pass through. Some stay. #### Loreborne [VERIFY: exact name in SRD]

The deep Mire has old stories in it, and Sallowreach has people who've been collecting them for generations. A Loreborne character might have come specifically for those stories — the founding traditions, the spirit accounts, the records of what the bayou was before the ward was built. They might find, uncomfortably, that what they thought they knew and what Sallowreach's keepers remember are not quite the same.

> *What drew you to studying the old records of this region? What were you hoping to find?*
>
> *What do the accounts you've read say about the Mire's spirit presence — and does it match what you feel here?*
>
> *Is someone specific expecting a report from you, or are you following your own thread?*

#### Seaborne [VERIFY: exact name in SRD]

The black water of the bayou isn't the open sea, but it speaks a similar language. A Seaborne character understands water, boats, and currents beneath the surface in ways that earn them immediate respect from Sallowreach's boatmen. The Wending — the navigable waterways through the Mire — is as complex as any coastline, and someone who can read water intuitively is genuinely valuable here.

> *What kind of waters have you navigated before? How does the Mire compare?*
>
> *The bayou changes — waterways shift, channels close, new ones open. Does that unsettle you or feel familiar?*
>
> *Sallowreach's boatmen have generational knowledge of the Wending. Do you try to learn from them, or trust your own instincts first?*

#### Druid

The Mire is a living ecosystem with undeniable spirit presence, and a Druid character exists at that intersection in ways that will be immediately meaningful. #### Ranger

Magic in Sallowreach exists in a complicated relationship with the Mages Guild's ward. A Wizard PC has an instant point of connection — or friction — with the wizard maintaining it. What school do you practice? What do you know about ward magic? What do you make of a Guild posting this isolated?

## PLAYER PRINCIPLES

*Share these with your players before session zero.*

#### Root Yourself

Invest in Sallowreach. Let its people matter to you. Eat the food, learn the names, participate in the celebration. The town's warmth is real — it's not a trick or a setup. Care about it. That care is the fuel for everything that follows.

#### These Strangers Aren't Your Friends Yet

You woke beside these people. You share an affliction — you're all stuck here, and none of you chose to be. You don't share a history. Let trust develop through play — through moments that earn it, through times they could have walked away and didn't. Don't skip to found family. Earn it.

#### The Swamp Is Not the Enemy

The Mire is dangerous. It is also wondrous, ancient, and following its own logic — one that doesn't map onto human categories of good and evil. Move through it with curiosity as well as caution. The marks on your skin came from somewhere in that water. Whatever put them there may not have meant you harm.

## GM PRINCIPLES

*Keep the following guidance in mind while running this campaign.*

#### Let Sallowreach Love Them First

Before the campaign asks anything hard of the party, let the town be genuinely good to them. Offer them food. Give them your best NPCs. Run the celebration with full warmth and joy. The harder moments only land if the players have something to lose. Build that investment before you pull any thread.

#### The Mire Takes Its Time

Never describe the swamp as only threatening. Give it beauty, strangeness, and the sense of something vast and patient — bioluminescence at night, the smell of brackish water and night-blooming flowers, the sound of channels shifting, the quality of light filtering through mangrove canopy at noon. The Mire has existed for thousands of years and will exist for thousands more. The party is temporary. The swamp is not.

#### NPCs Have Tuesday

The sixty thousand people of Sallowreach have lives that don't pause when the party isn't watching. When PCs ignore an NPC, that NPC keeps moving — pursuing their goals, forming opinions, taking action. Let the players occasionally arrive somewhere to find that things have already happened. The world is in motion.

#### The Devotion Is Real

The people of Sallowreach are genuinely, sincerely spiritual. Their belief in the honored guests is not superstition or folk performance — it is lived, inherited, practiced with care. When you portray the Celebration, portray it as the beautiful thing it is. When NPCs speak of the spirits, let them mean it. The horror of what the Celebration actually does only lands because the devotion behind it is completely authentic. Never let the irony make the faith seem foolish.

#### Complications Have Faces

Every problem in Sallowreach is attached to someone trying to do the right thing by someone else. Find the person inside every obstacle. Obstacles with faces are more interesting than obstacles that are just obstacles — and they give players something to negotiate with, appeal to, or be hurt by.

## DISTINCTIONS

*Use this information to prepare your campaign. Share what's appropriate with players during session zero.*

#### The Mire

The bayou is not background — it is a character. Travel beyond Sallowreach is by punt boat along [[The Wending]], the network of named channels locals have memorized over generations. The channels shift. New ones form; old ones close. A waterway that was safe last month may no longer exist. Outsiders get lost. Locals get lost less often.

The deep Mire — past the Wending, beyond the fishing grounds — is where even locals don't go casually. Navigation fails. Compasses spin. Animals behave strangely. The spirit presence thickens there, and something pays attention in a way that feels less diffuse, more directed.

#### The Honored Guests

Sallowreach has no priests, no scripture, no formal religion. What it has is an understanding — lived in the body, passed through families, never written down because it doesn't need to be. The Mire is full of spirits. How many? Unknown. What are their names? Unknown — it would be presumptuous to name something that hasn't named itself to you. Sallowreach calls them honored guests: presences sharing the space, deserving of welcome, not of commands or bargains or prayers.

This is not a passive relationship. The city takes hospitality to the spirits as seriously as hospitality to people. Offerings left at certain roots. Certain channels avoided after dark — not from fear, but from the same etiquette that keeps you from barging into a guest's room without knocking. If the spirits make themselves known, that is a gift. If they don't, that is their right. Sallowreach behaves well regardless.

The arriving party will be introduced to this framework quickly. They may even be called honored guests themselves, with genuine warmth. The irony of this is, at this stage, invisible to everyone.

#### The Celebration

Every week, without fail — the city's act of hospitality to its honored guests. Music before dawn. Chanting in old words. Dancing in patterns passed down through families, varying by district and lineage. Feasting at communal tables that run the length of major thoroughfares. Light-lanterns released over the black water toward the Mire — an open door, a weekly welcome.

Families have traditional roles passed down for generations. Newcomers learn the general forms within weeks; the family-specific traditions take years. The guild and imperial representatives are expected to participate. Those who refuse don't last long in their postings.

To participate in the Celebration is to belong, at least for an evening. The party will be welcomed into it. They should let themselves be — they won't fully understand what they're participating in yet, and neither does anyone else.

#### Mire Products

The alchemical ingredients, the extraordinary lumber, the spices and fibers and hides — these are what make Sallowreach worth the miserable journey to reach. Merchants who make the trek treat their Sallowreach contacts with care. The town sets its own prices. Goods move on Sallowreach's terms. This is a community that has never had to beg.

#### The Tether

The black water of the Mire is magically charged — old power, poorly understood, not something the ward was designed to address. People who go under in it come out changed. The party went under, and they came out bound to the Mire.

The Mire is accessible — all of it, including its deepest channels. But the roads that lead out of the Mire, the waterways that eventually reach open water and the wider world — those are closed. Something pushes back. You end up where you started. Sallowreach is a cage with extraordinary food and a genuinely lovely weekly celebration, which somehow makes it worse.

## THE INCITING INCIDENT

*Use the following to open your campaign, or adapt it freely.*

The ship broke apart somewhere in the Mire — a channel that shouldn't have been where it was, or a hull that had one bad plank too many, or something in the water that wanted it to happen. The party went under. The black water closed over them.

They wake on a muddy bank at the Mire's edge, coughing up silt, alive in the surprised way that means they nearly weren't. They do not know each other. They were passengers on the same vessel — that much is obvious — but strangers, not traveling together. There was no conversation beforehand worth remembering. There is now a tether worth noticing.

The first person who tries to leave feels it. A wall that isn't there — resistance, then something worse. They end up back where they started. The group works out what they already know: they can't leave the Mire.

The sun is mid-morning. Through the mangrove, Sallowreach is visible — boardwalk rooflines, cook-fire smoke, the distant sound of hammering. None of them planned to be here. All of them are here.

**[[Mira Solé]]** [placeholder — assign to whichever NPC the GM wants to make this first contact; a boatman on an early run, a healer's apprentice, a town elder with sharp eyes] comes along the bank path and stops when they see the wreck survivors. Their gaze moves across the group — taking in the wet clothes, the dazed expressions, the way none of them are standing quite far enough apart from the others. Something about that last detail registers. They offer to bring the party in, get them dry and fed, take them to the healer. They don't make a big deal of the distance thing. Not yet.

The party is on a doorstep they didn't choose. The only direction that makes sense is through it.

## CAMPAIGN MECHANICS

*The following mechanics are unique to this campaign.*

#### The Tether

The party is magically bound to the Mire. This is a hard rule of the world, not a condition to be managed.

The party can travel anywhere within the Mire — all channels, all depths, including places locals avoid. What they cannot do is leave the Mire system entirely. Roads that lead to the wider world, waterways that reach open water and away — these are closed. The resistance begins at the Mire's edge and becomes impassable. They have tried. Everyone tries once.

They can split up freely within the Mire. The curse is about leaving, not about each other.

**Lifting the Tether:**
The cure is in the Mire. The GM should not telegraph where or how early — let the party discover it through play. Full removal is the campaign's resolution condition.

#### The Wending (Mire Navigation)

Travel beyond Sallowreach into the Mire uses the following framework:

**With a local guide:** Travel is challenging but manageable. The guide handles navigation; the party handles threats.

**Without a guide — The Wending (known channels):** One character leads navigation using an Instinct roll [VERIFY: Daggerheart Action Roll mechanics and appropriate trait]. On a Hope result, travel proceeds. On a Fear result, the GM chooses: the party arrives late and drained (each PC takes 1 Stress), or they become **Lost**.

**Without a guide — Deep Mire (unfamiliar channels):** Navigation rolls are made at Disadvantage [VERIFY]. Being Lost in the Deep Mire means the GM draws from the Mire Complications table [see `tables/mire-complications.md` — to be built].

**Lost:**
The party must succeed on a second navigation attempt to find their way. Until they do, they cannot Long Rest [VERIFY]. Each hour spent Lost, the GM may spend 1 Fear for a Mire encounter.

## SESSION ZERO QUESTIONS

*Mark clearly which questions are for the table and which are private GM conversations.*

### For the Table (discuss openly)

> *What drew you to a bayou/swamp setting? What parts of that aesthetic most excite you?*
>
> *The town of Sallowreach is warm and welcoming — how do you feel about spending significant time in a social, community-focused environment between expeditions?*
>
> *The campaign is designed to have moments of genuine warmth AND emotional gut-punches. Is the table comfortable with both?*
>
> *Are there any content areas we should establish safety tools around before we begin?*

### For Each Player (private conversation with GM before session zero)

> *Where were you going when you boarded that ship? What were you traveling toward — a person, a place, an obligation, an opportunity? This is what the tether has interrupted. Your GM needs to know what's waiting for you elsewhere.*
>
> *Why does it matter that you get there? What happens if you don't make it?*
>
> *Who knows you were on that ship? Who is expecting you somewhere and will eventually notice you haven't arrived?*
>
> *What do you want from life that has nothing to do with being stuck in a bayou town with strangers? What were you hoping the next chapter of your life looked like?*
>
> *Is there anything about being stuck here — anything at all — that is not entirely unwelcome?*
