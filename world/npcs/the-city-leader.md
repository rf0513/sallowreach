# Adaline Roux

**Role:** Cultural chief and civic heart of Sallowreach; organizer, arbitrator, keeper of tradition
**Location:** [[Sallowreach]] — the Hall of the Mire, a stilted civic building at the city's center; also found wherever something important is happening, which is often
**Faction:** City Council / Old Families
**Tier:** 2

*She is not primarily a lawmaker. She is the reason Sallowreach feels like Sallowreach.*

---

## Appearance

A Black woman in her early sixties, broad-shouldered and unhurried, with the particular stillness of someone who has been the most important person in a room so many times that she no longer needs to announce it. Her hair is silver-streaked and worn up for official functions, loose when she's working — and she is always working. Her hands are expressive, her voice carries without effort, and she looks at people the way the Mire looks at strangers: completely, without apology, deciding something.

For the Celebration she wears the formal dress of the City Leader's office — deep Mire green, embroidered at the collar with the old families' patterns in gold thread, passed down through every person who has held this role. The rest of the time she dresses practically and well, the clothes of someone who will be outside in a bayou before noon and at a council table by evening.

One piece she wears always: a narrow braid of black cord around her left wrist, tied in the specific knot of the Roux family's outer boardwalk tradition. Her grandmother wore one. Her mother wore one. She's never taken it off.

---

## Personality & Mannerisms

She loves Sallowreach the way you love something you built — with specific pride, specific worry, and the permanent low hum of attention that never fully turns off.

**She is not hostile to outsiders. She is watchful.** There is a difference. She will welcome the party correctly, extend the honored guest tradition without irony, make sure they're fed and housed and understood to be under the city's protection. She will also be paying very close attention to what they do with that welcome. The Mire catches people for reasons. She wants to know what their reason is.

**She solves disputes the way a river finds a channel** — not by forcing a path but by making the right path easier than the wrong one. She listens to both sides longer than either party expects. She asks questions that make people reconsider what they actually want. By the time she says what she thinks, the room has usually already arrived there.

**She takes the Celebration personally.** She has organized it every week for twenty-two years. She knows every family's traditional role, every district's particular variation, every piece of the old form. She is not the Musicians' Accord and she is not a conservative about art — she has no objection to Fen, privately, as a musician. What she objects to is the idea that the Celebration could be casually changed by people who don't understand what it holds.

She doesn't know what it holds. She knows it holds something.

**She has opinions about the Empire and does not hide them.** She'll say them plainly, without heat — the heat has been burned off by twenty years of watching representatives cycle through. *"They send new ones every rotation. They call it a posting. I call it an audition."* She is not afraid of the guild, the Empire, or their money. She is afraid of the slow version: the year the old families can't afford the inner boardwalks, the decade the outer market becomes a guild district, the generation that grows up thinking Sallowreach is a place you invest in rather than a place you belong to.

**The swamp knows who belongs here.** This is her operating philosophy, stated simply and meant completely. People who disrespect the Mire fail in the Mire. People who approach it with honesty — with the specific humility of knowing you're in someone else's house — are treated accordingly. The honored guest tradition is built on this. She extends it to the party without hesitation: the Mire caught you, which is not an accident, which means the city owes you the welcome due to anyone the swamp has decided to examine.

She is right that the Mire is discerning. She does not know what it is discerning for.

---

## The Celebration

This is her domain more than any other. Not because she owns it — it belongs to the whole city — but because she is its living memory and its continuity. She knows which chants predate the written records. She knows which torch positions have never been changed and which were adjusted three generations ago after a flood. She knows the names of the drum-keeping families, the procession order, the specific moment in the evening when the canal-streets should go quiet before the water-lanterns are released.

She performs all of this with genuine devotion. It is not bureaucracy to her. It is the city's act of hospitality to something larger than itself, renewed every week, and she finds it beautiful.

*(GM SECRET: She was told, when she took office twenty-two years ago, what every City Leader has been told since the beginning: "The Celebration maintains our covenant with the presence that watches over the city." She understood "covenant" as mutual agreement — a compact of respect between Sallowreach and its honored guests. She has never questioned this. She has, occasionally, wondered why a covenant requires such precise repetition — why the specific words and patterns matter, why her predecessor told her that variation was not merely disrespectful but dangerous. She decided the precision was the point. That care, expressed in repetition, was what made it real. She has not looked further than this. She has not wanted to.)*

*(The document: somewhere in the Hall of the Mire, in a locked box passed from City Leader to City Leader, is a fragment of the original founders' record — water-damaged, partial, written in a hand two centuries old. It describes the Celebration's establishment to "hold the great presence in welcome and peace." "Hold" is the word. She has read it. She understood "hold" as "keep," as in "keep in welcome." The founders meant it differently. She does not know this. [GM: decide whether the party can access this document and what precisely it says — it should be legible enough to be a clue without being a confession])*

## What She Wants

Sallowreach to remain Sallowreach. Not frozen — she's not a preservationist. She knows the city changes, that it should change, that the outer boardwalk families have been part of the city's fabric for generations and the city's fabric shifts. What she is fighting, quietly and constantly, is replacement: the version of change that doesn't transform the city but substitutes it. Guild money buying the cultural infrastructure. Imperial aesthetics slowly overwriting the bayou ones. The Celebration becoming a tourist event rather than a civic act.

She is also fighting the outer boardwalk displacement directly — the Mire's expansion is swallowing the edge-families' homes, and the city's response has been too slow and too small, and she knows it, and it keeps her up.

---

## What She's Hiding

The doubt she's chosen not to pursue.

Twenty-two years of organizing the Celebration. Twenty-two years of the city leader's private knowledge — the founding document, the instruction about variation, the word "hold." Twenty-two years of something in the oldest chants that she cannot name but has felt, the way Delphine has felt something she cannot name, the way the outer boardwalk families have felt something without language for it.

She has not looked directly at the feeling. She has decided that her job is to keep the faith, not to interrogate it. That is what a covenant requires. You don't demand explanations from something older than your city.

She is not hiding this consciously. She has simply made a sustained, repeated choice not to examine what is right in front of her. The choice has become invisible from the inside.

What she will not say, even to herself: she is afraid that examining it would require her to change it. And changing it would mean deciding she understood something better than two hundred years of her predecessors. That kind of arrogance she is not prepared to claim.

## Campaign Function

**Act 1:** The party's formal introduction to Sallowreach's civic structure. She extends honored guest status — correctly, without warmth or coldness, the way you extend a serious tradition. She makes clear what that means: the city's protection, the city's welcome, and her personal attention to what they do with both. She is not hostile. She is watching.

She is also the party's first encounter with the Celebration as a civic act rather than a spectacle. If they participate — and she will notice if they don't — she will note it.

She will ask, at some point, what they were doing on that ship. Where they were going. She is trying to determine whether they're what they appear to be or advance work for something she can't see yet.

**Act 2:** She becomes a pressure point. The party is going deeper into the Mire, asking questions that touch things she's been carefully not touching — the ward, the Celebration's origins, the southwest pressure. She will not obstruct them directly. She will ask, with the specific politeness of someone with civic authority, what they're looking for and why.

She is also the person who, if the party has been building real relationships in the city — the outer boardwalk families, Théo, Remy — starts to believe in them. Not as tourists. As people.

**Act 3:** The hardest conversation in the campaign. The party has to look at the woman who has devoted her life to this city and this Celebration and tell her what both are built on. She will not believe them immediately. She will ask for evidence. She will ask the right hard questions. She will go very quiet when the evidence is sufficient.

What she says next determines which resolution is available. If she chooses to bring the truth to the city — Option C — she is the only one who can do it in a way the city will hear. The party can have Théo, Remy, Caspar, Sinope, Delphine — but Adaline Roux standing in front of Sallowreach and saying *"I need to tell you something about the Celebration"* is the only version of this conversation that stops sixty thousand people and makes them listen.

Whether she does it depends on who the party has been to her, and to this city, since they arrived.

## Dialogue Hooks

**On extending honored guest status:**
*"The Mire caught you. That's not an accident. Nothing in the Mire is an accident. You're here because you're supposed to be here, at least for now. The city treats that seriously. So do I."*

**On the Empire, plainly:**
*"They send new representatives every rotation. They call it a posting. I call it what it is — they're looking for the angle. The angle that makes us need them. They haven't found it in two hundred years. I intend to make it three."*

**On the outer boardwalk displacement:**
*"Those families have been on that water longer than the guild's been in this city. I will not preside over them being pushed to the edge of a map."*

**On the Celebration, if the party asks why the form never changes:**
*"Because the form is the point. Care expressed in repetition is what makes it real. You don't honor something by deciding you understand it better than everyone who came before you."* [a beat, something underneath the certainty] *"You just keep faith. That's what a covenant asks."*

**On the party's presence, if they've been asking too many questions:**
*"You've been here three weeks. There are families on this boardwalk who've been here two hundred years. Be careful what you assume you understand about this place."*

**On the swamp:**
*"The Mire knows. I know that sounds like a thing people say. I've watched it my whole life. People who come in here treating it like a resource fail. People who come in treating it like a house they've been invited into — they find their way. The swamp is discerning. It always has been."*

**If the party brings her the truth:**
Very still. Then: *"Show me."* Not angry. Not dismissive. The voice of someone who has been deciding not to look at something for twenty-two years and has just been handed what was always going to be there when she did.

---

## Stat Block

Not a combat NPC. If the city is threatened, she is organizing the response — every resource, every relationship, every family that owes the city and every family the city owes. She does not fight. She is, in her own way, considerably more dangerous than someone who does.
