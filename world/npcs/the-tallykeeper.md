# The Tallykeeper

**Role:** Psychopomp of the Mire — the ferryman wearing a heron
**Location:** Wherever something is dying. Lately: one roofline from [[Isotta]], and always one roof closer than last time.
**Faction:** Independent — older than every faction in the city, and most of the city's ground
**Tier:** 4 *(age and office, not a stat block — see below)*

*Mire folk have a superstition: you never chase a heron off a sickroom roof. Herons stand where people die. This is why.*

---

## The Titles

Spirits don't carry names the way people do — they *accumulate* what each era calls them, layered like sediment, and **the count of titles is the weight of the spirit** (see [[Cosmology]]). Basile has one name and wears it like a favorite coat. This one has six, and each is a stratum you can date:

1. **The Tallykeeper** — what the dead of Sallowreach call it now. The first name Isotta will hear, and only from a ghost.
2. **The Ferryman** — what the living half-remember at sickbeds and funerals; the root the roof-heron superstition grows from.
3. **Grand-Gris** — the punt families' fond old name. Theirs is the polite *good morning* a passerby gives the man-tall heron on the piling ([[wandering-sights]] — that entry was always this).
4. **The Knotter of Cords** — the title in the oldest punt-family papers in [[Caspar Renne]]'s archive, from when the city was pilings and prayer.
5. **The Even Wake** — the nearest translation of what the [[Tessak]] call it. Even the man-eaters leave it be: it comes for their dead at the same price as everyone else's, and they respect a fair scale.
6. **The First Courtesy** — the oldest title, used only by other spirits. It bows to everything it meets, because the crossing is the one debt owed to every living thing — and it has never once been rude about collecting.

**As a clue chain:** each community can surface one title in play. When Isotta's casebook holds all six, [[Vael Miroux]]'s weighing-lesson lands on its own: *"Count the names, girl. That's how you weigh a spirit."* Six is not a haunting. Six is a fact of the world, like the tide.

## Nature

It did not cross the veil out of curiosity or loneliness, the way small spirits do. It is part of the **machinery of crossing** — the office, wearing a bird. This is why the salt wall means nothing to it: the wall was built to keep spirits *out*, and the Tallykeeper is not an intruder. **The wall can't repel it any more than a door can repel its own hinge.**

It keeps the Mire's ledger: a cord and a knot for every death, tied at the crossing, two hundred years of tidy lines and countless more before anyone wrote anything down. In all that time it has missed exactly one — [[Isotta]]'s, snatched back mid-crossing by the transformation ritual. Her cord hangs unknotted, worn smooth from handling. An uncollected death is a flaw in the order of things. It does not rage at this. It does not grieve it. It *attends* to it, the way a tide attends to a shore.

## Appearance

**Living side:** a great grey heron, still as a nail driven into wood, always at the edge of sight — a roofline, a channel post, the far end of a funeral. It inclines its head when noticed. Courteously. Like an old man greeting someone he knows.

**Veilside:** the height of a stilt-house, feathers like wet slate, and around its neck on a cord hangs the **ledger of knots** — hundreds upon thousands of small tidy deaths, each one tied off and finished. One cord hangs unknotted and smooth. When Isotta looks at it, it lifts toward her, softly, like a compass needle finding north.

## Personality & Mannerisms

Formal, unhurried, incapable of menace *on purpose* — which makes it eerier: it will state its intention to take her with the same courtesy it uses to bow. It speaks (veilside only, and only when it decides the account is ready to discuss) in the language of tides and bookkeeping: things owed, things carried, things that must balance. It does not understand haste and is genuinely baffled by fear of it. It has waited two hundred years. It can wait more. It is the *waiting* that unsettles.

**Delivery tic for the GM:** it never uses contractions, and it talks in short, ruled lines — like ledger entries. Slow. Say a sentence, let it land, small pause, next one. That's the whole performance; no spooky voice needed.

## What It Wants

**To collect her. Not someday — as soon as it can.** The cold arithmetic that drives the whole arc:

- She cannot die again — her death already happened. Instead she **frays**, and if she frays to nothing there is nothing left to collect: no crossing, no knot, a line that stays open **forever**. The one entry it cannot make.
- The ritual's tie blocked collection — but the tie is fraying along with her, faster here where the veil runs thin. When it wears through enough to sever, it **will** collect her, wrong-shaped and late, because a late collection still closes the line. Dissolution never does.
- The only outcome it prefers to collecting her now: her becoming **properly mortal again**, living a life, and dying a collectible death at the end of it. The ledger doesn't need her death today. It needs her line *closeable*.

So it can be bargained with — but the bargain is never "life or death, pick one." The bargain is **time.**

## The Grace Period

When it introduces itself (timing in GM Notes), it states its business plainly: it has come to collect, while there is still enough of her to take. In its own words — deliverable as written:

> "Two endings balance your line. You cross now. Or you make yourself mortal again — live your life — and I collect you at the end of it. Like everyone else. Either works.
>
> The third ending does not balance. You wear through to nothing. Nothing to collect. An open line, forever.
>
> So. Convince me to wait."

Her one move is to negotiate for time to fix herself — and time, to a ledger, is **risk**. She has to make the risk worth carrying:

- **Collateral:** her closed [[isotta-arc|Lantern Docket]] cases are the currency — proof she balances accounts. Each closed case is an installment against her own.
- **The grace cord (table prop):** it ties a cord with **six loose loops** around her wrist. Whenever her Slipping clock fills, one loop draws tight; each closed docket case loosens one back. **When the last loop closes, it collects — ready or not.** Hand the player an actual cord. `[VERIFY — loop/clock ratio at the table; adjust freely]`
- The grace is revocable arithmetic, not kindness. If her fraying accelerates past the value of waiting, the deal is off — and it will tell her so, politely, in advance.

## What It's Hiding

- **It will not fly over the heart of the city.** Even it is kept out of there. *(One line, said once, never explained — this is its only strand into the great binding, and it stays a strand.)*
- **It has been standing watch while Isotta sleeps** — and her fraying slows when it's near. This is not tenderness. She is the only thing in two hundred years it could *lose*; it is protecting the asset. (And yet: her cord is worn smooth from handling, and it returns to it more often than the others. The ledger would deny this means anything. **Never explain it.**)

## Key Relationships

- [[Isotta]] — the open line; the only error in an eternity of clean accounts. Not prey. Not a target. *Owed.*
- [[old-patience|Old Patience]] — **a different being entirely**: the Still Pool spirit, a neighbor of comparable age. They keep a companionable distance. The Still Pool is one of the few places the Tallykeeper has never needed to visit — nothing dies there.
- [[Vael Miroux]] — has never once raised a blade at it in forty years of hunting. It knows. It considers this correct.
- **Anselme Thouvenel** ([[session-04-salt-wall]]) — an account not yet ready; it does not rush him. The dead revere it the way sailors revere the tide.
- **The city's heart** — see What It's Hiding.

## Dialogue Hook

> "I do not hunt, little debt. I wait. There is a difference. You, of all people, are owed the difference."

And at the introduction, when she asks if it's come to take her:

> "You misunderstand me. I am not deciding whether to take you. I am deciding whether I can afford to wait. Help me with the sums."

## Parley & Etiquette

Distinct from Old Patience's still-water protocols — the Tallykeeper's etiquette is an *accounting*:

1. **Always return the bow.** It offers courtesy first, every time. Failing to return it doesn't anger it; it simply re-files you under *not ready*, and the conversation waits — possibly years.
2. **Speak plainly.** You cannot lie to a ledger. Attempted bargaining-by-omission just gets read aloud back to you, in full, which is worse.
3. **Owe nothing you don't mean to pay.** Promises made to it are *entries*. It will not warn you about this, because it cannot imagine needing to.

- **Endgame is a menu, not a script** — the live options (step back into life / out-of-grace renegotiation / choose the crossing / the player's invention) are in [[isotta-arc#Endgame — a menu, not a script]].
- **Clue delivery:** the Secrets & Clues list in [[isotta-arc]] is the drip-feed; the six titles above are a parallel, countable clue chain for a mystery-loving player.
- **Tone guardrail:** never menacing, never comic. Courteous, patient, absolutely unmoved — the dread comes from the arithmetic, not the delivery. If the players catch the worn-smooth cord and start arguing about what it means, never confirm either way.
