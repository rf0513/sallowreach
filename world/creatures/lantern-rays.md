# Lantern Rays

**Type:** Mire creature, keystone species
**Location:** [[The Mire]] — deep channels throughout, with highest concentrations along the southwest Wending; school near the surface at dusk
**Tier:** Non-combat

*The city releases lanterns on the water every Celebration. Nobody remembers why. This is why.*

---

## The Creature

Flat, wide, manta-like — the body of something built for gliding rather than swimming, though they do both. A fully grown lantern ray spans roughly two arm-lengths across. Their undersides are pale, almost white. Their dorsal surface is deep Mire-green, near-black in low light, and runs a bioluminescent stripe from the base of the head to the tip of the tail that pulses blue-green in slow, rhythmic waves. When a school synchronizes — which they do, instinctively, in proximity — the pulses travel through the group like a single heartbeat. Dozens of rays, one rhythm. A constellation that breathes.

They are silent. Completely silent. No call, no click, no sound of movement through water. Their first appearance is always light before shape.

They school at dusk, rising from the deep channels where they rest during the day. A school of twenty is common. Fifty is extraordinary. On certain nights in certain seasons the southwest Wending produces schools of two hundred or more, and the water lights up for half a mile.

**They have never learned to fear anything.** Nothing in the Mire hunts them — they are too large for most predators, too fast for the rest, and the bioluminescence that would seem to mark them as targets instead functions as a warning the Mire's predators have learned to respect over centuries. They approach punts. They approach people standing still in the water. They will hover at arm's length and regard you with small, dark, lateral eyes, and the hovering carries no threat — only the specific quality of something that is genuinely curious and has never been given a reason to be otherwise.

Remy Duval has a personal relationship with what he estimates are several recurring individuals in the southeast Wending. He has names for three of them. He will tell you about them at length.

## First Appearance — The Shipwreck

*This is the party's first encounter with lantern rays. It should be their first experience of Mire wonder — occurring in the middle of their worst moment.*

The ship goes down at dusk. The timing is not a coincidence in the sense that matters — the Mire doesn't care about the ship — but dusk is when the rays rise, and the ship goes down in their channel, and that is what happens.

The party goes into the black water. This is when the tether forms — the spirit-charged water doing what it does to people who enter it. They are disoriented, possibly injured, surrounded by the chaos of a sinking ship in darkness. The water is cold and absolutely black and there is no way to know which direction leads up.

Then: the water lights up.

The school of lantern rays below — disturbed by the commotion, by the impact, by whatever goes into the water when a ship sinks — rises. Not toward the ship. Away from it, toward shallower water, toward shore. This is what they do when disturbed. The Mire didn't choose to help. The rays aren't helping. They're just doing what lantern rays do when something large crashes into their channel at dusk.

**From above:** The black water illuminated from beneath. Moving light, blue-green, pulsing in slow rhythm, spreading outward from the wreck site toward shore. If you didn't know what it was, you would think the Mire itself was breathing.

**From below** — from the party, disoriented in the water: surrounded by moving light. The rays don't touch them. They move around them, past them, toward the shallows. But their direction is legible. Their light is enough. Enough to see the surface. Enough to see which way Remy is reaching.

**The personal beat:** One PC — whoever was deepest, whoever came closest to not making it — opens their eyes in the chaos underwater. A lantern ray is right there. Hovering. Glowing. Regarding them with that unhurried lateral gaze. The PC follows it up. The GM should identify this player before the session and let the moment be theirs specifically. They don't have to explain it. The ray was just there. They're alive because they followed the light.

**Remy's role:** He's amphibious and in the water immediately, pulling people toward the emergency raft. In the dark, in the chaos, the rays are his navigation — he follows their movement toward the shallows, and the survivors he's pulling come with him. He does this without explaining it. He just does it. Later, if the party asks how he found them, he'll say: *"The rays were moving. The rays always know where the shallow water is."*

**What to plant in this scene:**
The party's first impression of the Mire is black water and terror and something being done to them they don't understand. And then light. Living light, rising from below, moving with purpose that turns out to be survival. File this feeling. The campaign will return to it.

## The Celebration Connection

*GM Only.*

Sallowreach has released lanterns on the water during every Celebration for two hundred years. Paper lanterns, candlelit, floated on the canal-streets and the outer channels at the height of the evening. It is one of the most beloved elements of the ritual.

Nobody alive knows where the tradition started. Caspar Renne has a record from the founding era that describes "lights on the water, as the Mire gives them." He has interpreted this as an early description of the lantern release itself — the founders documenting a tradition that already existed.

He has it backwards. The founders were describing what they saw in the Mire and choosing to echo it. The city has been commemorating the lantern rays for two hundred years without knowing what it was commemorating.

When the party makes this connection — and a party paying attention will make this connection — it should land quietly. The city loves these creatures and has been saying so every week for two centuries. They just forgot to say it in a way anyone could understand.

## Ecological Role

*GM Only.*

Lantern rays are a keystone species. Their role in the Mire's ecosystem is not obvious, because it operates at a scale and depth that casual observation doesn't reach.

During their nightly feeding runs in the deep channels, they disturb and aerate the sediment in ways that allow specific deep-channel plants to germinate. They also carry spores on their bodies — a passive transport mechanism, not intentional — spreading those plants throughout their range. The specific plants they support: the luminescent algae, the deep-root moss, the channel fungi that Sallowreach's alchemical ingredient trade depends on.

The connection has never been documented because nobody has looked for it. The ingredients grow. The rays exist. Nobody has studied whether these facts are related.

They are related. Remove the rays and the deep-channel plants begin to fail within one growing season. Within two seasons, the rare alchemical ingredients stop regenerating in the channels. The Alchemists' Consortium's supply dries up. Sallowreach's second most valuable export collapses.

Sallowreach has never experienced resource depletion. The lumber never runs out — the [[Tree Spirit]]'s essence replenishes it continuously, though nobody knows this. The city has developed a foundational assumption: the Mire's resources are effectively inexhaustible. This assumption is wrong about everything except the lumber, and nobody has ever had cause to test it.

They are about to.

## The Side-Plot — Taking Beyond the Season

*GM Only. Runs across Acts 1–2, resolved before or during the Deep Mire sequence.*

### Gault's Operation

[[Gault]] discovered the lantern rays six months ago. More specifically, he discovered that their bioluminescent organs produce an oil that alchemical institutions pay extraordinary sums for — stable, long-lasting, magically active in ways that synthetic alternatives aren't. He had a buyer within a month. He had a workforce within two.

He cannot run the operation alone. Lantern rays are fast, non-aggressive, and extremely good at evading a single extractor. You need people in the water from multiple angles simultaneously. He needed a workforce that could operate in the Mire without permits, without questions, and without the city knowing.

He found the Tessak.

### The Tessak

A lizardfolk clan that has lived in the outer Mire's deep channels for generations. Not a large community — perhaps sixty adults, their young, their elders. They know the Mire in ways Remy doesn't know it, because they live inside it rather than navigating it. They have channels they've used for a hundred years. They have a relationship with the lantern rays that predates Sallowreach by centuries.

They also have a word — *tessavrak* — that translates approximately as "taking beyond the season." Their own concept of overpoaching, developed across generations of living in a finite ecosystem. The elders use it as a warning. The clan has always understood that the Mire gives back only what is taken with restraint.

They are doing tessavrak right now. They know it. They are doing it anyway.

**Why:** A sickness has been moving through the clan's youngest members for eight months. Something in the water chemistry of their home channels — altered by the Mangrove Spirit's growing pressure on the Mire's ecosystem — has been affecting their young disproportionately. They've lost four children. More are sick.

Gault arrived with medicine sourced from Sallowreach's outer market and a job offer. He didn't know about tessavrak. He didn't ask. He offered what they needed and they said yes, because what else do you do when your children are dying.

**Drevath** leads the Tessak delegation working with Gault. They speak enough common tongue to negotiate and to give orders. They are not comfortable with what they're doing — the elders have been clear about what tessavrak means for the channel ecosystem — but Drevath has children among the sick and has made a choice that the elders understand even as they oppose it. When the party first encounters the Tessak, Drevath will not be apologetic. They will be doing what they have to do. The shame is visible anyway, underneath.

### Separating the Tessak from Gault

The Tessak can be separated from Gault without violence. They have no loyalty to him — only to the medicine he provides and the deal they've made. If the party can offer what they actually need, the deal ends.

**What they need:** Treatment for the sick children. [[Delphine]] can do this. The water chemistry issue she cannot fix — the Mangrove Spirit's pressure on the ecosystem is beyond her — but she can treat the symptoms, stabilize the sick, and give the clan time to find their own solutions. She will do this without hesitation if the party explains the situation. She may have opinions about what the Tessak have been doing. She will treat the children anyway.

The party may also be able to advocate with [[Adaline Roux]] for the Tessak's situation — the Mire's non-human inhabitants are not part of Sallowreach's formal concern, but they are part of the Mire, and Adaline's philosophy about the Mire as a shared home has implications she hasn't fully examined. This is a harder path but has longer-term benefits: formal recognition of the Tessak as a Mire community, access to Sallowreach's resources through legitimate channels.

**When the deal ends:** Gault loses his workforce. Without the Tessak, he cannot run the extraction at scale. He continues alone — stubborn, greedy, accelerating toward the Deep Mire and whatever he thinks is the source of the concentrations. The operation slows dramatically. The rays get a reprieve.

### The Collapse Timeline

If the operation continues unchecked:
- **End of Act 2:** The lantern ray population in the southeast Wending is visibly reduced. Remy notices. He cannot explain it. He files it.
- **Beginning of Act 3:** The deep-channel plant regeneration begins to fail in the most heavily extracted areas. Alchemical ingredient gatherers report unusual scarcity. The Consortium's supply becomes irregular.
- **Mid-Act 3:** If Gault is not stopped and the Tessak not separated, the alchemical ingredient trade enters crisis. Sallowreach's leadership, lacking any framework for this, is baffled. Maren documents it.
- **Unchecked beyond campaign end:** Full ecological collapse of the alchemical ingredient ecosystem. The trade is gone. Sallowreach is, for the first time in its history, economically vulnerable in a way the lumber trade cannot compensate for.

Sallowreach will never connect this to the lantern rays without the party's help. The party can choose to tell them. This requires explaining the concept of overpoaching to a city that has never needed the concept, which is its own kind of scene.

## The Three Encounters

### Encounter 1 — Dusk on the Wending *(Early Act 2)*

The party is on the water at dusk — headed somewhere else, this is not the point of the trip. Remy goes still.

*"Everyone stop punting. Right now. Don't — yes. Do you see that?"*

The school rises ahead of them. Thirty rays, maybe more, emerging from the deep channel and gliding toward the surface. The synchronized pulse begins as they school — one rhythm, the water lit from below, blue-green spreading outward. One ray peels from the group and approaches the punt, hovering at surface level, regarding the occupants with its small dark eyes.

Remy is completely overcome. He narrates in a hushed voice that somehow conveys the same energy as shouting. He has wanted to show a client this for years. He knows their range, their habits, their approximate population in this channel. He has been watching them for twenty years.

*"She's done this before — see how she approaches from the starboard side? That's territorial curiosity, not aggression. She wants to know if you're food. You're not food. She'll figure that out in about — there. See? She's decided."*

The party falls in love. This is the point.

Then: on the far bank, in the reeds — nets. Recently used, not yet retrieved. Harvested remains at the water's edge. The school is smaller than it should be for this section of the Wending. Remy goes quiet in a specific way.

He counts. He counts again.

*"This school had forty-three individuals last month."*

He doesn't say anything else for a while. The party should feel the shift without being told what it means.

They won't catch anyone today. The operation moves on the water faster than they can follow without knowing where it's going. But they have a face for the problem now: nets, remains, a school that's smaller than it should be.

### Encounter 2 — The Tessak Camp *(Mid-Act 2)*

Following the nets, following Gault's trail, following the extraction damage further southwest — the party finds the camp. A Tessak staging area on a Mire island, partially hidden, with Gault's waterproofed containers stacked and waiting for transport. The Tessak are there. Gault may or may not be — he moves constantly.

Drevath meets them at the water's edge before they can land. Not aggressive — assessing. They've been expecting something like this. The city sends people eventually.

The conversation should be uncomfortable in the right way. The Tessak are not villains. Drevath is not performing innocence — they know what tessavrak means, they're doing it, they've made a choice they're not proud of. They will tell the party about the sick children if pressed, without self-pity. They will tell the party what Gault is paying them. They will not apologize.

*"You would do the same,"* Drevath says, and the party should not be entirely sure they wouldn't.

If the party offers an alternative — Delphine, Adaline, some path to what the Tessak actually need — Drevath will be very still for a moment. Then: *"Bring what you say you can bring. We will wait."* They will, in fact, wait. Drevath's word is good even when the situation it's given in is not.

If the party comes with threats instead of alternatives: the Tessak don't back down, and Gault's containers are rigged to sink if the camp is raided. He thought of this. He's been doing this for eight years.

### Encounter 3 — The Deep Mire

This is the Gault fight. See [[Gault]] for the full encounter. The Tessak are gone by this point — separated from the operation if the party gave them an alternative, or present but staying out of it if the situation resolved without their full buy-in.

What changes now that the rays are central: the Deep Mire in this section is extraordinary bioluminescent territory. The lantern rays don't come this deep normally — but the concentrations Gault has been chasing, the same ones drawing him toward the Mangrove Spirit's territory, produce something that resembles the rays' natural light in the deep water. Gault has been following what he thinks is a massive undiscovered school. He's wrong about what it is. He is in entirely the wrong place for entirely the wrong reasons, and the party catches him there.

## GM Notes — The Through-Line

The lantern rays appear four times in the campaign. Run them as a coherent thread:

**Shipwreck:** First experience of Mire wonder in the worst moment. The party is alive partly because of where the rays were at dusk. Plant this without underlining it.

**Remy's love:** On the first proper Mire trip, Remy shows them. Full Steve Irwin. The party meets the rays properly — curious, unafraid, magnificent. This is the moment they become attached.

**The nets:** Discovery of the poaching. The party sees what someone has been doing to the thing they just fell in love with. The shift in Remy's face. Don't over-explain. Let the math be visible.

**The Celebration:** At some point — ideally after the party has engaged with the side-plot and before the truth about the Celebration is revealed — someone notices that the lanterns released on the water look exactly like the rays. This can be a player observation or a GM note dropped in passing. Either way: the city has been honoring these creatures every week for two hundred years without knowing it.

When the party understands what the Celebration actually is — a prison — that detail about the lanterns should be somewhere in their minds. The city built something beautiful in unconscious memory of something beautiful. The city is also unknowingly keeping a spirit captive. Both things are true. Both things are Sallowreach.

**Thematic note:** Sallowreach has never learned the concept of resource depletion because the Tree Spirit's essence has made the lumber inexhaustible. The lantern ray side-plot is the lesson in miniature: something finite can be taken past the point of return, and a city that has never experienced that has no framework for recognizing it until it's too late. The party learns this lesson here. It's the kind of understanding that tends to matter when they reckon with the main plot — what the spirits' situation costs, and what restraint or sacrifice the resolution might ask of them.

The Tessak have a word for it. *Tessavrak.* Taking beyond the season.

Sallowreach doesn't.
