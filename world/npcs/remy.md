# Remy Duval

**Role:** Innkeeper of The Honored Guest; Sallowreach's only Mire guide for civilian clients
**Location:** [[Sallowreach]] — The Honored Guest Inn, Mire-edge district, southwest boardwalk
**Faction:** Independent
**Tier:** 2

*Remy was on the shipwreck. He was the one who booked the passage, who organized the trip, who was supposed to get these people safely to Sallowreach. He came up out of the black water alongside them. He is, at this moment, the only person any of them know.*

---

## The Honored Guest Inn

The inn was named generations before Remy was born. The tradition of honored guests runs through Sallowreach's founding — the name is old. Remy didn't choose it. It suits him anyway.

The common room is hung with things he's pulled from the Mire or been given by clients: a deep-water lamp of impossible design, three mounted specimens of creatures that have no formal name, a hand-drawn map of the Wending that contradicts the official one in several important ways and is correct where they differ. The food is exceptional — he learned to cook from his grandmother, who learned from hers, in the specific lineage of Sallowreach cuisine that treats Mire ingredients as the foundation rather than the garnish. The rooms are small and kept well. The beds are better than the inn looks like they'd be.

There is a punt mounted above the bar. Not decorative. It's his first one, retired after a collision with a submerged root that should have killed him and didn't. He left it up as a reminder of the kind of luck you don't get twice.

---

## Appearance

Remy is a frog person — broad, barrel-chested, with the wide stance of someone who has never once thought about their balance because their balance is simply excellent. His skin is mottled brown and deep amber with a pale throat, the coloring of something that belongs in the Mire, which he does. His eyes are large and very expressive and when he is excited about something — which is often — they go slightly wider and the effect is somewhere between endearing and unsettling depending on how you feel about frogs.

His hands are broad with grip pads at the fingertips, which makes him extraordinary with a punt pole and gives him the ability to hold onto wet surfaces that would defeat a human grip entirely. He doesn't towel off aggressively after going in the water. He doesn't need to.

He dresses practically but with one deliberate piece: a long vest in deep Sallowreach red, embroidered at the hem with Mire-grass patterns by a client he brought out alive about eight years ago. He wears it for every Mire trip. It is his thing. He is aware it is his thing.

When he's in the inn, he fills the room in the way good hosts do — present everywhere, drink always right, remembering the shoulder you hurt last week. When he's on the water, something shifts: very still, very focused, the specific stillness of someone reading the Mire the way other people read text. Both versions are completely genuine. He does not experience them as different.

## The Business

He has a monopoly on civilian Mire access and is mildly embarrassed by it when pressed. Everything else moving in and out of Sallowreach is trading vessels, guild transports, or fishing boats — none of which take passengers without a very good reason. Remy takes passengers. He runs maybe eight, ten trips a year, word of mouth only, and the inn covers the rest. It is not a large operation. He would not describe himself as successful so much as *self-sustaining and doing what he likes.*

Sallowreach locals find this eccentric. Not in a cruel way — he's well-liked, does no harm, keeps a good table — but the city is not built for outsiders and most of its residents cannot understand why anyone would keep seeking them out. The running joke is that Remy collects strangers the way [[Caspar Renne]] collects documents. Both of them take this as a compliment.

## Personality & Mannerisms

He leads with warmth and follows with competence, in that order, which is the correct order. The warmth is genuine — he likes people, likes hearing where they've come from and where they're going, and remembers details about guests long after they've left. The competence is also genuine, and slightly more frightening when you see it up close.

**The danger is part of the branding, and the branding is true.** He will describe the most terrifying stretch of the Wending with obvious delight, not because he's performing confidence but because he finds it genuinely wonderful that a thing can be that dangerous and that beautiful simultaneously. *"That section — I call it the Hinge, because the channel turns in a way that makes no hydrological sense, and if you're not watching the water you go into the mangroves at speed. First time I ran it I lost a paddle. Third time I lost a client's hat and the client nearly went with it. Now I know the Hinge. The Hinge doesn't surprise me anymore."* [beat] *"I named it the Hinge."*

He names things. The official Mire charts have formal designations; Remy has twenty years of his own nomenclature layered over them, and his names are more useful. Locals who work the Wending use his names half the time without knowing it.

**He is, about the Mire's creatures, completely Steve Irwin.** He has individual names for at least a dozen recurring animals he's encountered over the years. He will stop a trip mid-channel because something interesting just surfaced and everyone needs to see this. He handles dangerous Mire fauna with the calm of someone who has been bitten enough times to have developed opinions about which bites are worth it. *"Oh — oh, everyone stop punting. Do you see that? That's a ridgeback crawler. I've been trying to get a client close to one for four years. Don't move. She's just curious. Isn't she magnificent."* He is not being rhetorical. He genuinely thinks she is magnificent.

**He is, about survival situations, completely Bear Grylls.** When something goes wrong in the Mire — and things go wrong in the Mire — a different register comes on: clipped, practical, no wasted words. He has already assessed the situation and formed a plan by the time most people have registered that there is a situation. He will eat things in the Mire that will make his clients stare. He will explain, while eating them, why this is the correct choice given current conditions. He is not wrong.

**He has opinions and expresses them at a slight angle** — living in Sallowreach requires not making enemies of the wrong people, and he's been here long enough to know who the wrong people are. He'll tell you exactly what he thinks about the salt wall deterioration, but he'll phrase his thoughts about the wizard in a way that's technically about the Guild's maintenance protocols rather than Oswin specifically. He makes this distinction less carefully after the third drink.

**He takes the honored guest tradition seriously.** He's hosted three sets of honored guests in his years running the inn. Two left. One didn't. He doesn't talk about the one who didn't. When the tethered party arrives, he treats their status with genuine weight — not performance, not hospitality theater. He looks them over, gets them inside, gets them fed, and starts asking the right questions to understand what they're actually dealing with.

## What He Wants

To be taken seriously about the Mire. He's been filing informal reports through every channel available to him about the southwest wall degradation for fourteen months. He's been told, repeatedly, that the ward is performing within acceptable parameters. He knows what the wall looked like two years ago and he knows what it looks like now, and he knows the difference between "performing within acceptable parameters" and "noticeably thinner than it should be." He is not satisfied. He does not know who is responsible. He suspects the Guild.

He also wants to go back into the Deep Mire. He won't admit this, including to himself. But it's there — the unfinished quality of something left undone in a place he hasn't returned to.

---

## What He's Hiding

Three years ago he took a client into the Deep Mire. A merchant named Seve Orans, who was looking for something specific and wouldn't say what, and paid triple rate for the privacy of not being asked. Remy brought them in. Seve found what they were looking for, or found something — Remy heard the shout from the punt but by the time he pushed through the growth the merchant was gone. The water was still. Remy spent six hours searching and came out alone.

The death was ruled an accident, which is what all unexplained Deep Mire deaths are ruled. Remy has never publicly questioned this because it's probably true. He also cannot entirely shake the sense that Seve found something that should not have been found, and that the Deep Mire made a decision about it.

He has not taken a client into the Deep Mire since. He hasn't turned down a request directly — there have been no requests. But this party will need to go there, eventually, and he knows it, and he's been aware of it since they walked in the door.

He also knows where the Still Pool is. He will not take clients there. Once, six years ago, in the early morning on the southwest channel, something organized in the water near his punt — the bioluminescence arranged and held, and he felt the specific sensation of being examined by something very old. He held very still. It passed. He has thought about it at least once a week since and told no one.

## Campaign Function

**The opening:** Remy saves the party. The ship goes down in the Mire — Remy, amphibious and completely at home in the water, gets the emergency raft secured and pulls people out. He is in the water but never submerged in the way the party is; his frog nature means he's working the surface, not the depths. The party goes under and comes up marked by the spirit-water. Remy comes up wet and already thinking about next steps.

He is not tethered. He doesn't fully understand why the party is behaving strangely in those first hours — one of them tries to leave and ends up back where they started — but he clocks it quickly, asks the right questions, and gets everyone to the inn before anything else.

Once they're inside: he's their translator. He knows what honored guest status means and takes it seriously — he's hosted three sets of honored guests in his years at the inn, and the weight of it is real to him regardless of what the skeptics say. He knows who the party should talk to and in what order. He answers every question they have about Sallowreach, not as exposition but as the natural conversation of someone who loves his city and finally has people who have to listen to him talk about it.

He connects them to [[Delphine]] with a specific referral: *"Her practice is on the Thornside boardwalk, third door past the red lantern. Tell her I sent you. She'll actually look at you."*

**As their guide into the Mire:** He runs the Wending well and his clients feel it. He names things as they go. He reads the water in a way that becomes quietly impressive once the party has seen him do it three or four times. The tether being loosened by Old Patience changes the logistics of what he can do with them.

When the party needs to go into the Deep Mire: he goes. He doesn't explain what it costs him. If the party has been paying attention, they'll be able to tell.

**As a voice the city trusts:** He's not a political actor, but he's spent twenty years building relationships in Sallowreach and the city trusts him. If the party ever needs a character witness, a voice that the outer boardwalk families will listen to, a person who can stand up in front of the City Leader and say *I have been in that Mire, I have seen what it's doing, and these people are telling you the truth* — Remy can do that.

## Dialogue Hooks

**On the raft, getting people out of the water:**
*"You — grab this. Hold on. I've got you."* [pulls someone up, immediately looks for the next one] *"Count off when you're on. Count off."*

**Once everyone's accounted for, punting toward the city:**
*"Don't drink the water. I know you already did. I'm saying don't do more of it."*

**Once they're inside and dry, first drink in hand:**
*"I want to be honest with you: I've never lost a ship before. Cargo, yes. A punt once. Not a ship."* [beat, genuinely processing] *"You're all here, which is the part that matters. But — the thing in the water. What did you feel when you went under? Tell me exactly."*

**In the Mire, spotting something:**
*"Oh — oh, everyone stop. Do you see that? Don't point, just — yes. That's a ridgeback crawler. I've been trying to get a client close to one for four years. Don't move. She's just curious."* [reverent pause] *"Isn't she magnificent."*

**On something dangerous, practical register:**
*"Okay. What's happening right now is [situation]. The correct response is [action]. I need you to do that right now and ask questions after. Ready? Go."*

**On the Mire, to a new client:**
*"The Wending is navigable. I have navigated it approximately four hundred times. I know every channel, every root, every spot where the current does something the water has no business doing. I will not tell you it's safe, because it's not. I will tell you that with me, you will most likely be fine. That's an honest offer."*

**On the salt wall, after a third drink:**
*"I've walked that wall two hundred times in the last year. I know what 'performing within acceptable parameters' looks like. That's not what that looks like."*

**On the Deep Mire, if pressed:**
*"I know where it is."* [pause] *"I haven't taken anyone there in three years."* [doesn't explain further unless directly asked, and even then, not fully]

**On the Celebration:**
*"I've been in this city my whole life. I have done the Celebration — the chanting, the dancing, all of it — since I was old enough to stand. Whatever else it is, it is also real. Don't let anyone tell you it isn't."*

## Quests

### The Ridgeback Crawler *(Act 1 — Steve Irwin mode)*

Remy has been trying to properly document a ridgeback crawler for six years. He's seen one twice. He knows roughly where it nests — a shallow basin off the southeast Wending that requires four people to approach quietly from different angles, otherwise it submerges and doesn't come back up for hours. He has never had four people patient enough to attempt this.

He asks the party like it's a favor he's slightly embarrassed to request. It is not the most urgent thing they have going on. That's the point.

**What actually happens:** The crawler is there. The approach works or it doesn't depending on the party's patience, which Remy will assess quietly and without judgment. If they get close: he is completely overcome with professional joy, barely contained, narrating in a hushed voice that somehow conveys the same energy as shouting. The creature is large, strange, beautiful, and has no interest in harming anyone as long as everyone stays still.

The basin they're in is further southwest than Remy usually takes clients. The bioluminescence here behaves oddly — not the crawler's doing. The Mire is pressing in this direction. Remy notices this but doesn't say anything. He files it.

**Reward:** Remy's genuine affection and the specific kind of trust you build with someone by being patient about something they love. He starts treating the party less like clients and more like people. He also knows the Mire better than anyone — after this trip, he'll answer deeper questions than he would have before.

---

### The Southwest Corner *(Act 1–2 — Bear Grylls mode)*

Remy has been walking the salt wall for fourteen months and filing informal complaints through every channel available to him. He has been told everything is within acceptable parameters. He wants witnesses — people who can say they saw what he saw, so he stops being the eccentric frog guide with a grudge against the Guild.

He also wants a second set of eyes on the southwest corner specifically. Something is wrong with the erosion pattern there. Salt ward erosion is irregular but consistent — Mire pressure isn't methodical. What he's seeing at the southwest corner is methodical. It's not random wearing. Something is testing that section.

*He doesn't know what that means. He just knows it's different from the rest of the wall and he can't explain it.*

**What the party finds:** The wall is visibly thinner than it should be — even a non-expert can see the difference between the northeast sections (properly maintained) and the southwest face (running on fumes). At the corner Remy flags: the erosion pattern is almost regular. Rhythmic. Like pressure applied and released and applied again over a very long time by something with patience and intention.

This is the Mangrove Spirit pressing against the ward. It has been doing this for longer than [[Oswin Cray]] has been here. The ward is the only thing between its longing and the city, and it has been leaning on the weak spot for decades. Nobody has connected this to spirit behavior because nobody has been looking for that connection.

**What the party does with this information:** Up to them. They now have firsthand evidence that the wall is failing and that whatever is on the other side is not random. Remy doesn't have the framework to interpret the methodical erosion yet — but the party might, especially after they've talked to Caspar or Delphine.

**Reward:** The observation becomes a key piece of evidence. Also: Remy is vindicated, and he is quietly, genuinely relieved that someone else saw it. He will remember that the party took him seriously when the Guild didn't.

### Seve Orans *(Act 2 — his secret, trust-gated)*

This quest doesn't exist until the party has earned it. Remy doesn't offer it — it surfaces in a late-night conversation at the inn after the party has been into the Mire with him at least once and he's decided they're real.

He tells them about Seve. The merchant who paid triple rate for privacy, who found something in the Deep Mire and didn't come back. He tells it simply, without self-pity, which makes it worse. He says: *"I need to go back to where it happened. I've needed to for three years. I can't do it alone and I haven't trusted anyone enough to ask."*

He's not asking for justice or closure in any tidy sense. He wants to know what Seve found. He suspects it matters.

**What they find:** The site of Seve's death is in the Deep Mire, past the Wending, in territory that's gotten more active as the Mangrove Spirit's longing intensifies. There's no body — the Mire takes what it takes. But there are traces: the remains of a camp, a cached journal wrapped in oilcloth that Seve left before going further, a deliberate marker that reads like someone who expected they might not return.

Seve's journal describes looking for evidence of a spirit presence deep in the Mire — not the Mangrove Spirit by name, but *something large and old that the water follows.* Seve was a researcher, not a merchant. The cover story was pragmatic. The journal doesn't conclude — the last entry is mid-sentence.

This is not the Mangrove Spirit that killed Seve. Seve went further than the Deep Mire encounter site, deeper than Remy knew, following something that moved. What happened after the last journal entry is unknown and should remain unknown. Some things the Mire keeps.

**What this does:** It gets the party and Remy into the Deep Mire for a real reason before the main quest requires it. Remy confronts his guilt and comes out the other side of it. The journal confirms that others have been drawn to the spirit presence — that this is not new, that something in the Mire has been attracting attention for longer than the city has existed. And Remy, having done this, is ready to take the party further when they need it.

**Reward:** Remy, fully. Whatever he was holding back, he stops. He'll go into the Deep Mire for the Mangrove Spirit encounter without being asked twice. He's been preparing for that trip for three years without knowing it.

### A Boat of Their Own *(Act 1 — standing pressure, not a one-off)*

Remy's boat went down with the ship — and the wreck was sabotage ([[Caius Maren|Caius]]'s doing, unknown to anyone yet). He's gutted but functional, and too proud to take charity; he'd rather earn a new one *with* the party than be handed it. Until then, getting into the Mire means **renting a punt at [[The Mooring]]** — a constant coin-bleed on a tethered, penniless party, with the deep channels (where the cure lives) locked behind a boat they can't yet afford.

This is less a quest than the economic spine of Act 1: rent the leaky skiff → take jobs → save toward a used boat (the bleed ends), then the proper Deep-Mire craft (Act 2 opens, and it's the boat that reaches [[The Mangrove Spirit's Heart]]). The money fork — honest ([[Théo Rouchard|Théo]]), dirty ([[Marcel Dray|Marcel]]), or poisoned ([[Caius Maren|Caius]]) — is the choice that makes the pressure produce story. **Full system, prices, and the dock-boss [[The Mooring#Sabine Aucoin|Sabine Aucoin]] live in [[The Mooring]].** Let the party restore Remy, not rescue him.

He does not panic.
