# The Drowning Edge

**Tier 1 Traversal / Event**

> *"The Lethaby porch went under last spring. Old Sera still sweeps it. You can hear the broom on the boards at low water, scraping at silt that was never going to come off."*

**Type:** Traversal / Event — the southwest outer boardwalk, where [[Sallowreach]]'s edge meets the [[The Mire|Mire]]'s advance. This is the ground-level face of **Front 3 (*The Edge Erodes*)** in [[world-in-motion|the world in motion]]: not a battlefield, a slow flood and a slower eviction. The danger is failing structure, rising water, and the human cost of a relocation in progress. Drop it in when the party walks the southwest edge, helps a family move (or refuse to), or crosses the boardwalk after the salt-ward [[Oswin Cray|degraded another notch]]. Pair with [[Oswin Cray]] (the thinning wall is just beyond the last houses) and [[Adaline Roux]] (Adaline's response that everyone, herself first, calls too slow).

**Impulses:** Take the edge inch by inch, make the families choose between home and safety, reach toward the city through the gaps in the boards, file every lost house as one more loss no one will answer for.

**Difficulty:** 11

**Potential Adversaries:** Rarely needed — this is a place of water and rot, not enemies. If something comes in on the tide: a Mire beast displaced from a drowned basin — [[mire-bestiary#Bloodmidge Swarm|biting swarm]], glass snake (SRD) — or scavengers and salvage-thieves stripping evacuated homes ahead of the families (use [[sallowreach-roster#Wending Runner|Wending Runner / Lackey]] stats). The real "adversary" is the water and the clock.

## Features

**The Tide Line - Passive:** A short examination tells the whole story. A PC who studies the southwest edge — the salt crust crumbling where it used to hold, the high-water stain climbing the stilts, the houses with windows already pulled for salvage — may make an **Instinct Roll** (or **Knowledge**, if they reason it out from the structures). [VERIFY: trait and Action Roll framing per the campaign frame] On a **success with Hope**, they read it precisely: how far the water has come this season versus last, which houses are already empty and which are stubbornly not, and roughly how long the standing ones have. On a **success with Fear**, they get the shape of it but miss something — a floor that looks sound and isn't, a family that's "gone" but hasn't actually left. On a **failure**, they see only a poor stretch of waterfront and underestimate every danger here by one step. *Whatever they learn, frame it through a name, never a statistic — Adaline's instinct: "a person, not a status update."*

**Rotten Boards - Action:** The boardwalk is giving way underfoot, and the worst spots aren't marked. When the party moves at speed, carries weight (furniture, an elder, a child), or crosses a stretch the GM has flagged as far gone, the lead PC makes an **Agility Reaction Roll** or the planks let go: a drop into waist-deep black water below the walk, **1d6+1 physical damage** from the broken boards and the fall [VERIFY: damage scaling for Tier 1], and anything carried — salvage, a rescued keepsake, a person being helped across — goes into the water unless another PC spends their action to catch it. The water here is shallow and foul, not deep and hungry; the danger is the cold, the cuts, and what you dropped, not drowning. [VERIFY: Reaction Roll]

**Evacuation Countdown - Action/Reaction:** When the party commits to getting a household out — possessions, people, or both — before the next high water or a worsening surge, run a **Progress Countdown (6)** [VERIFY: Progress Countdown size for the scope of work], advanced by PCs hauling, carrying, punting loads to higher boardwalk, talking a frightened family through the door. A **roll with Hope** advances it 2 and saves something that mattered. A **roll with Fear** advances it 1 but costs: a load goes into the water, a board gives way (trigger *Rotten Boards* free), or the family balks again at the threshold. If the Countdown completes before the water turns, they get everyone and most of it out. If the water turns first, see *The Surge*. *The GM may spend a Fear to shorten the time the party has — the surge comes sooner than the tide promised.*

**The Holdout - Reaction:** Somewhere on this edge is a porch that's half in the water and a person who will not leave it — sweeping boards no one will walk again, refusing the relocation order, "I was born on this water and I'll go off it when it takes me, not when the city says." This is a roleplay reaction, not a roll: the family refuses, and the party must decide whether to carry them out against their will, talk them out with dignity intact, leave them, or stay. There is no clean answer. [[Fen]] is often here — she played a defiant set on a porch like this the night the first relocation order went up, because nobody asked her not to. She'll be playing again. *Decide what the holdout would rather lose than admit.*

**The Surge - Action:** *(Fear-gated escalation.)* Spend a Fear: the water doesn't rise, it *reaches* — a section of boardwalk collapses outright and the black water surges up through the gap, faster and higher than any tide should run. Every PC in the affected span makes an **Agility Reaction Roll**; on a failure, **1d6+1 physical damage**, swept and moved Close toward open water, and **Vulnerable** until they get clear and find footing. [VERIFY: Reaction Roll, Vulnerable condition] Anything left on the collapsed span — salvage, a load not yet hauled, a person not yet across — is now in the water and going under. This is the [[The Mangrove Spirit|Mire]]'s longing made physical: not anger, *reaching*, the same pressure that's thinning [[Oswin Cray|the wall]] just beyond the last houses. Never name it. Let them feel the edge give.

**Something on the Tide - Action:** *(Fear-gated escalation.)* Spend a Fear when the water is high and the party is occupied with the evacuation: something displaced from a drowned basin comes in with the surge — a biting swarm boiling out of a flooded crawlspace, a glass snake [VERIFY: SRD Beast] coiled where the porch meets the water, or human scavengers who came to strip an empty house and found it not quite empty [VERIFY: SRD stat block]. It takes the spotlight at Close range. It is not the point of this place — it's the complication that makes saving the household cost something.

## Feature Questions

*Which PC has a reason to care that this specific stretch of water is winning — and what did they promise, or fail to promise, the people who live on it?*

*When the family at the holdout porch refuses to leave, what does the party value more: the people's safety, or the people's right to choose how they lose their home?*

*What does the party carry out of a drowning house first when there's only time for one load — and who decides?*

*The night the relocation order goes up, [[Fen]] is playing on the edge. Does the party listen, join, ask her to stop — or notice that the water likes her music?*

## Running This

The Drowning Edge is **the campaign's grief made small enough to hold.** Fronts 1 and 2 — the rising spirit, the thinning wall — are abstractions until a family loses the home their great-grandmother built, and this is where that lands. Run it for **dignity, not squalor.** These are proud people losing ground their family won outright; the tragedy is erasure under a slow tide, not poverty. Sera sweeps a porch that's underwater because that porch is hers and the sweeping is a refusal. Play that, not pity.

**Most of the time this is low-stakes and quiet — Difficulty 11, a Passive read of the tide line, a conversation on a sagging porch.** Don't reach for the Fear-gated features unless the moment earns weight: an evacuation that has to beat the water, the first time a section gives way under someone the party knows, the night the order goes up and the families are deciding whether this city is still theirs. *The Surge* and *Something on the Tide* are for when you want the edge to genuinely take something — spend the Fear, drop the boards, and let them feel the Mire reaching through the gaps. That reaching is the same force the whole campaign turns on; here it just looks like a flooded kitchen and an old woman's broom.

**Tie it to the world in motion every time.** Each lost house is, secretly, document N+1 in [[Caius Maren]]'s file and one more thing [[Adaline Roux]] couldn't get to fast enough — but the party only sees the family. Show them the wall thinning just past the last stilts ([[Oswin Cray]]'s southwest line) and let them draw the connection themselves. And **wait on the placeholder.** This environment is only fully armed once the edge-family has a name, a face, and a thread back to someone at the table — until then, run it on the Lethaby scaffolding and keep the real slot open.
