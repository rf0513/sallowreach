# The World in Motion

*GM Eyes Only. The "default timeline" — what Sallowreach is already doing, with or without the party.*

---

## How to Read This File

This is **not a doom clock** and **not a schedule.** Nothing here is fated. This file describes the **momentum** the party is dropping into — the forces already in motion, who drives them, and the direction they tend to drift when no one intervenes.

Use it as a set of **dials, not a track.** Each session, look at where the party spent their attention and turn the dials accordingly:
- **Ignore a front** → advance it a notch. The world moved while they were elsewhere.
- **Engage a front** → it bends. Players redirect, accelerate, stall, or merge these threads constantly. Let them.
- **Never tick everything at once.** Advance one or two fronts a session, usually the ones the party *brushed against but didn't resolve.* That's what makes the world feel like it's paying attention.

The fronts **feed each other** (see *The Interlock* below). Turning one dial often nudges another. That coupling is the engine — lean on it.

When player action makes a stage here obsolete, **rewrite it.** This document is a starting position, not a constitution.

---

## Why Now? — The Equilibrium Has Tipped

The most important thing to understand: **Sallowreach was stable for two hundred years, and it is not stable anymore.** The party arrives at the exact moment the old balance is failing. That's not coincidence — it's the premise.

For two centuries the system held: the [[Cosmology|binding]] kept the [[Tree Spirit]] imprisoned, the [[Cosmology|Celebration]] quietly reinforced [[Oswin Cray|the ward]], the ward held back the [[Mangrove Spirit]]'s reaching, the extraordinary lumber paid for everything, and everyone who might have noticed had a reason not to look. A perfect, self-perpetuating wrong.

Four things broke it, all landing in the same generation:

1. **The grief crossed a threshold.** The Mangrove Spirit's longing isn't constant — it compounds. Two centuries of accumulating grief have finally begun to exceed what the ward can hold. This was always going to happen *eventually.* It is the floor under everything else.
2. **[[Oswin Cray]] skimmed the ward to ~60%** of its maintenance budget, right as the pressure peaked.
3. **[[Caius Maren]] arrived and began actively sabotaging it** — denying the budget, *wanting* the wall to fail.
4. **[[Fen]] found the wrong note** — the one alteration that frays the binding — and keeps playing it louder.

The thematic spine: the ancient grief was a slow fuse, and **three small human failings — embezzlement, ambition, and art — pulled the reckoning forward from "someday" to "now."** Mortal pettiness is bringing a spirit's grief to a head.

So when a player asks *"why doesn't the town just do this forever?"* — the answer is: **it was about to. It can't anymore. And nobody inside the city understands why.**

## The Interlock — The Engine Under Everything

Buried in the existing material, easy to miss, load-bearing: **the binding and the ward are the same clock.**

[[Oswin Cray]] has noted that the ward "always feels more settled the morning after" the Celebration — a maintenance effect he can't reverse-engineer. The [[Cosmology]] confirms the salt-ward "was built in relationship to the binding." So:

> The weekly ritual that imprisons the Tree Spirit *also* quietly reinforces the wall holding back the Mangrove Spirit.

Consequences the GM should run on instinct:
- Every time **Fen** frays the binding, she also weakens the ward. The two are coupled.
- **Caius** thinks he's engineering "a bad season" by failing the wall. He's pulling a thread that, past a point, unravels a 200-year prison.
- Anything that disrupts the Celebration has a **physical** echo at the southwest wall the next morning.

The party will not know this for a long time. The GM should know it from session one.

## The Fronts

Each front is a dial. Format: what it is, who drives it, where it stands at the start, how it drifts if ignored, how players can bend it, and the tells you can drop at the table.

---

### Front 1 — The Grief Rises *(the deep engine)*

**What it is:** The [[Mangrove Spirit]]'s longing intensifying. The Mire's growth is not aggression — it is a vast thing reaching for what was taken from it.

**Driven by:** The spirit itself. Geological time, but compounding.

**Where it stands now:** Accelerating. The growth is measurably faster than it was a decade ago. This is the pressure behind every other front.

**If left alone:** It does not stop. It cannot — the thing it reaches for is still imprisoned. This is the one front the party can't simply *halt;* they can only address its cause (free or reunite the spirits) or watch the pressure climb.

**Player levers:** Almost none, directly — and that's the point. The grief is weather, not an enemy. The only real lever is the cause: the [[Tree Spirit]]. Everything else is managing symptoms.

**Tells at the table:** Boardwalks choked with roots that weren't there last season. Bioluminescence pulsing in patterns that track nothing in the wind. Channels narrowing. The sense, in the deep Mire, of something vast turning its attention — not toward the party, *through* them, toward the city.

---

### Front 2 — The Wall Thins *(the coupled physical clock)*

**What it is:** The salt-ward on Sallowreach's southwest face degrading — from underfunding, from rising grief, and (secretly) from the binding fraying.

**Driven by:** [[Oswin Cray]] (neglect), the rising grief (Front 1), and any disruption to the Celebration (the Interlock).

**Where it stands now:** Visibly thinner than a year ago. The southwest outer-boardwalk families have noticed. The city has filed two formal inquiries; Oswin answered both with technically-true, strategically-incomplete reports. Running at ~60% of intended maintenance.

**If left alone:** It keeps thinning. The southwest edge keeps losing ground (feeds Front 3). Past a threshold, it breaches — and a breach is not a flood of water, it's two centuries of compressed longing pouring in (see *The Gravity Well*).

**Player levers:**
- *Slow it:* expose or relieve Oswin's situation so the ward gets properly funded; restore the skimmed budget; protect the Celebration.
- *Accelerate it:* disrupt the Celebration without understanding the Interlock; remove Oswin without a replacement; help Caius (unknowingly).
- *Redirect it:* learn the Interlock and use it deliberately — a controlled weakening becomes a tool rather than a catastrophe.

**Tells at the table:** Salt crust crumbling where it used to hold. Oswin checking the southwest line twice a week, more tired each time. A family on the edge whose floor is now ankle-deep at high water. The morning after a Celebration, the wall *humming,* briefly solid again — and no one able to say why.

---

### Front 3 — The Edge Erodes *(the human face — your visible clock)*

**What it is:** The outer-boardwalk displacement. Where the abstract — spirit grief, ward physics, imperial scheming — becomes a family losing the home their great-grandmother built.

**Driven by:** Fronts 1 and 2, made worse by [[Adaline Roux]]'s response being "too slow and too small" (her own assessment) and by [[Caius Maren]] quietly filing every lost home as evidence.

**Where it stands now:** The first relocation orders have gone up on the southwest edge. A handful of families displaced; more in the path. The city's response is underfunded and reactive.

**If left alone:** The edge keeps retreating. Displacement compounds into a constituency of the angry and the dispossessed (feeds Front 4). Each lost home is document *N+1* in Caius's file (feeds Front 6).

**Player levers:**
- *Slow it:* pressure the city to act faster; help shore the wall (Front 2); win Adaline's trust enough that she spends real capital here.
- *Redirect it:* make the displacement *visible* in a way the old families can't ignore — turn a slow erosion into a civic reckoning.
- *Personalize it:* tie a named edge-family to a PC. This is the front most worth attaching to a specific face. *(Build the family when you know your players — hold for Session Zero.)*

**Tells at the table:** A posted relocation order, ink running in the damp. A boardwalk house with its windows already pulled for salvage. An old man refusing to leave a porch that's half in the water. [[Fen]] playing a defiant set on that porch the night the order went up — because nobody asked her not to.

---

### Front 4 — The Fracture *(the civic fault line)*

**What it is:** Sallowreach splitting along a seam it's always had but never had to look at — the outer-boardwalk families, the late-shift workers, and the shut-out artists on one side; the Musicians' Accord, the old guard, and the comfortable inner districts on the other.

**Driven by:** [[Fen]] (the natural voice of the dispossessed) and the displacement (Front 3) that keeps feeding her audience.

**Where it stands now:** Latent but warming. Fen has been rejected by the Accord three times and plays to the holdout families on the outer water. The displacement is giving her grievance a constituency it never had.

**If left alone:** The seam widens into a real divide. The old guard reads the unrest as disrespect for tradition; the edge reads the tradition as a club that was never theirs. [[Adaline Roux]] — who has "neither shut Fen down nor protected her" — loses control of the narrative.

**Player levers:**
- *Widen it:* take a side loudly; champion Fen against the Accord; expose old-family hypocrisy.
- *Bridge it:* broker between Fen and Adaline; give the edge families a win that doesn't require a villain.
- *Weaponize it (carefully):* a fractured city is easier for Caius to "rescue." A united one is his nightmare.

**Tells at the table:** Two letters of concern framed on Fen's wall. An Accord member describing her haircut as "unfortunate" to her face. An inner-district table going quiet when an outer-boardwalk family sits down. The sense that the Celebration — the thing that's supposed to make everyone one city — is starting to feel like *whose* city.

---

### Front 5 — The Wrong Note *(the wild card / the accidental cure)*

**What it is:** [[Fen]], who found by accident the one rhythmic alteration that disrupts the binding, playing it louder and to bigger crowds — with no idea what she's actually doing.

**Driven by:** Fen's ambition, the Fracture (Front 4) that's radicalizing her, and the Interlock (every performance frays binding *and* ward).

**Where it stands now:** She plays her arrangement on the outer water and has noticed the bioluminescence "prefers" it. She reads this as the Mire liking her version. She is both wrong and very nearly right. She's told no one.

**If left alone:** She escalates — a defiant uninvited set at a Celebration, or louder waterside shows backed by an angry edge. The binding cracks in public, and *nobody understands why.* Strange phenomena bloom: lumber going ordinary, water shifting, the Celebration "feeling wrong." [[Adaline]]'s inherited warning — *variation is dangerous* — appears to come true, which entrenches the old guard *against* Fen and deepens Front 4. A cracked-but-not-broken binding is more volatile than a sealed one.

**Player levers:**
- *This is the campaign's most dangerous gift.* Fen is an accidental key the party can recognize before she does.
- *Arm it:* once the party knows the truth, Fen is the one person who can perform the disruption deliberately, in public, with sixty thousand witnesses and none of them the wiser.
- *Defuse it:* keep her offstage, or get her the legitimate slot she wants on terms that don't crack the binding by accident.
- *Race it:* if the party dawdles, Fen may pull the trigger herself, blind — turning a tool into a disaster.

**Tells at the table:** Water-light shivering to a drumbeat. Fen, quieter than usual: *"You'd have to see it. It only does it with my arrangement. Not the traditional. Just mine."* [[Caspar Renne]] mentioning the old words predate written history — a fact Fen filed away and forgot.

---

### Front 6 — The Patient Man *(Caius's clock)*

**What it is:** [[Caius Maren]] engineering the crisis that justifies imperial annexation — and not knowing he's engineering a catastrophe instead.

**Driven by:** Caius, running out of patience. Two years in, thirty-two years old, one incident short of his petition.

**Where it stands now:** He has arranged the shipwreck (the party's), denied [[Oswin Cray]]'s budget request, and built a two-year incident file. He waits for the wall to fail. He believes he's manufacturing a manageable flood. He has no idea what's actually on the other side of that wall.

**If left alone:** He waits, watches, and files. When the wall breaches (Front 2), he moves to "help" — petition in hand. But the breach is past imperial management (see *The Gravity Well*), and everything he did was unauthorized. He's gambling the capital ratifies success; if it's catastrophe instead, he's a rogue agent who arranged a massacre.

**Player levers:**
- *Expose him:* [[Marcel Dray]] holds the shipwreck connection and will hand it over to the right instrument. Exposure before he files leaves him with no imperial protection.
- *Outrun his clock:* the capital will eventually rotate or audit him. Stall him long enough and *his own side* defuses him — without touching the grief underneath.
- *Feed him (mistake):* a fractured, failing city is exactly the crisis he needs. Disunity is his fuel.

**Tells at the table:** A handwritten dinner invitation, warm and formal. Questions about the shipwreck a shade too specific. His name, later, on the paperwork denying Oswin's funds. The mask, when cornered: *"You have no idea what you've walked into. This city is already decided."*

---

### Front 7 — The Holstered Gun *(Marcel's trigger)*

**What it is:** [[Marcel Dray]], waiting. He holds two weapons — the evidence to disgrace Oswin (and collect his debt) and the proof of what Caius did — and he is sitting on both, looking for the right instrument.

**Driven by:** Marcel's patience, which is real but not infinite.

**Where it stands now:** Holstered. He waits for a clean instrument (the party are an excellent one). Absent that, he does nothing — Oswin keeps paying in silence, Caius's secret stays filed.

**If left alone — his trigger:** Marcel loves this city in his cold way (back of the Celebration every week, anonymous contributions to the feast tables). **Decide his line:** when the slow collapse threatens his operation or his home, he stops waiting. He may deploy the Caius evidence himself, force Oswin's hand directly, or quietly fund the ward to keep his city standing. A patient man with a stake is still a man who acts when the stake is threatened.

**Player levers:**
- *Be his instrument:* come to him with the right question and he hands over what he has, no second ask.
- *Cut him out:* find Oswin's evidence yourselves (it's in the Maintenance House) and you never owe Marcel anything — and he doesn't need to know.
- *Beat his clock:* resolve the collapse before his trigger trips, and his guns stay holstered — a loose end, not a player.

**Tells at the table:** A cold handshake that spreads up the wrist. Knowing the party's names before they give them. *"I deal in what's collectable. The wizard isn't — yet."*

---

### Front 8 — The Steward's Blindness *(Adaline's slow choice)*

**What it is:** [[Adaline Roux]] managing decline — competent, devoted, and exactly one degree too cautious to look at the thing in the city's heart.

**Driven by:** Adaline's 22-year practiced choice *not to examine* what the Celebration holds, the founding document's word "hold," and her fear that confronting it would destabilize the one wall between the city and the southwest.

**Where it stands now:** She knows the ward is degrading and has done "nothing sufficient." She knows the displacement is outpacing the city's response. She keeps the faith because keeping the faith is what she believes her job to be.

**If left alone:** She holds the city together as it slowly loses its edge — the tragic steward, doing everything right except the one impossible thing. The decline continues on her watch, and she carries it.

**Player levers:**
- *Open her eyes:* she is the hardest and most earned conversation in the campaign. Bring her the truth with evidence she can't wave off and she'll go very still — *"Show me."*
- *Earn her:* whether she'll ever act on the truth depends entirely on who the party has been to her and to the city. She is the one person who can stop sixty thousand people and make them listen — if she chooses to.
- *Lose her:* push too fast, assume too much, and she closes — *"Be careful what you assume you understand about this place."*

**Tells at the table:** A black cord knotted at her wrist, never removed. The pause under her certainty when asked why the form never changes: *"You just keep faith. That's what a covenant asks."*

---

### Front 9 — The Grieving Mother *(the party's own thread — session 3)*

**What it is:** A mother owlbear whose two cubs were trafficked out of the uplands through [[Gault]]'s Mooring pipeline. One cub the party wounded, saved, and left recovering with [[Brin Sevarre]]; the other is [[Marcel Dray]]'s pet. She is following their scent toward Sallowreach.

**Driven by:** grief and instinct. Owlbears do not forget their young.

**Where it stands now:** Distant, coming. The smuggler who took the cubs is dead (see [[Marcel Dray]]), so no one manages her but the Mire itself.

**If left alone:** she reaches the city — a Tier 2+ storm of talons and grief — and the only thing that calms her is the cubs. One's with Brin; one's in Marcel's quarters. Getting the second back means going through Marcel.

**Player levers:** reunite her with the cub(s); intercept her before she reaches the boardwalks; or let her arrive on her own terms. Returning Marcel's cub is a lever *on Marcel* as much as on her.

**Tells at the table:** a far-off answering call the night the cub was hurt; trappers' tales of something huge moving up the southwest channels; the saved cub going still and crying toward the dark at the same hour each night.

**Thematic rhyme:** a vast grieving parent reaching toward stolen young, kept apart by human greed — the [[Mangrove Spirit]]'s whole story, scaled to fur and talons. The party meets the small version long before they understand the large one.

## The Gravity Well — One Trajectory If No One Ever Intervenes

This is **not the ending.** It is the direction the fronts drift when nothing pushes back — the gravity the party is perturbing the moment they wash up on the bank. Useful for knowing which way "downhill" runs, so you can feel where player action bends the curve.

1. **The edge erodes.** Southwest families lose homes inch by inch. The city's response lags. Caius files every loss.
2. **The fracture opens.** The displaced radicalize. Fen becomes their voice. Old guard versus edge hardens.
3. **The wrong note, loud.** Fen cracks the binding in public, blind. Phenomena bloom; nobody understands; the old guard turns *on* the edge; the binding goes volatile.
4. **The breach.** Frayed binding + underfunded wall + peaking grief give out. And it isn't a flood — it's two centuries of compressed longing pouring in, not in anger but in *reaching.* The outer boardwalk is consumed.
5. **The empire inherits ash.** Caius files mid-catastrophe, but the catastrophe is past managing. The capital finds a disaster zone and a rogue agent who engineered a massacre. He's disavowed, or he drowns in his own flood. **And the cruelest beat: the Tree Spirit is still bound, in the Hall of the Mire at the city's *heart,* not its edge. So even after flooding the outer city, the Mangrove Spirit still hasn't found its companion. The grief doesn't stop. The longing just keeps coming.** The love story never resolves, because no one ever knew it was a love story.

The "do nothing" world is a city that **destroys itself by refusing to look at the thing in its own heart.** That's the gravity. The party is the thing that can break its pull.

## Why Only the Party Can Bend This

Worth keeping in front of you, because it's *why the campaign needs players.* Everyone inside the system has a structural reason not to assemble the truth — and the knowledge is **scattered, one fragment per person, none of whom can move between the others:**

- [[Oswin Cray]] has the ward physics but won't research, because researching means caring again.
- [[Caspar Renne]] has the only transcription of the old words but no reason to tie it to a flood.
- [[Delphine]] *feels* it but can't name it.
- [[Adaline Roux]] holds the founding document that says *"hold the great presence"* — and has chosen for 22 years not to look at the word.
- [[Fen]] found the cure-note by accident and thinks the swamp just likes her.
- [[Marcel Dray]] has the Caius evidence and is sitting on it.

Each holds one piece. Assembling them requires someone who can move freely between the crime boss, the wizard, the museum, the swamp, and the spirits themselves — **an outsider, tethered here, motivated to understand, with no local reason to look away.** The world cannot save itself; it is structurally blind. That blindness is the tragedy the party walks into — and the reason they matter.

## Using This at the Table — Quick Procedure

1. **After each session,** glance at the eight fronts. Which did the party touch? Which did they ignore?
2. **Advance one or two** — usually the ones they *brushed but didn't resolve.* Note the new state in the tracker below.
3. **Honor the Interlock.** If they disrupted the Celebration, thin the wall. If they shored the wall, the edge holds a little longer. Pull the coupled dials together.
4. **Let players redraw fronts.** If they invent a solution nobody wrote down (they will), rewrite the affected fronts to match. This file serves the table, not the reverse.
5. **Keep faces on it.** When a front advances, show it through a *person* the party knows — a displaced family, a tired Oswin, a defiant Fen — not a status update.
