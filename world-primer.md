# Sallowreach — Player's Guide

---

## The Pitch

The Arcana Empire runs on magic. Magic runs on materials. The best materials in the known world come from a single place: the Mire of Sallowreach, a bioluminescent swamp at the edge of the map, surrounding a city of sixty thousand that has never had to ask permission for anything and has never had to beg.

You were on your way somewhere else. The Mire had other ideas.

*Sallowreach* is a Daggerheart campaign about being stranded in the most interesting place you've never wanted to be — finding community in a city that didn't ask for you, navigating a swamp that is actively paying attention, and learning that a place this old has stories buried in it that the people living there have long since stopped being able to see.

---

## Tone & Feel

**Warm.** Sallowreach is genuinely good to you. The food is extraordinary. The people are proud and interesting and real. Let yourself care about them.

**Eerie.** The Mire at night is bioluminescent and ancient and watching. Beauty and unease are the same feeling here.

**Communal.** A city of sixty thousand with roots four generations deep and a weekly celebration that gets under your skin whether you want it to or not.

**Melancholic.** Everyone here is carrying something. So are you. This campaign earns its heavy moments.

**Wondrous.** The swamp does not follow human rules. Neither does what you'll find in it.

**Urgent.** You were traveling somewhere. Someone is waiting. The Mire doesn't care.

---

## Themes

**Separation & Longing.** You had somewhere to be. Someone is expecting you. The Mire has cut you off from a life you were building, and the gap it leaves is specific and personal — not abstract loss, but the particular shape of the thing you can't get back to.

**Community & Its Costs.** Sallowreach is a beautiful, functioning community with deep roots and real warmth. It also has things buried in it that its people have chosen, generation after generation, not to look at too closely. Being an outsider gives you eyes they don't have.

**What We Inherit.** The traditions here are old. The knowledge passed through punt families and woodworking guilds and celebration-keepers is genuinely precious and genuinely earned. The question worth asking is: what else has been passed down without question?

**Nature's Own Logic.** The Mire is not hostile. It is not tame. It operates on a scale and by rules that have nothing to do with human convenience, and it will not apologize for this. Move through it with curiosity as well as caution.

**Who You Are Away From Home.** Stripped of context, stuck somewhere new, surrounded by strangers you didn't choose: who do you turn out to be? What do you reach for? What do you protect?

## Touchstones

*The Wizard, The Witch, and The Wild One* — Brennan Lee Mulligan. The template. A campaign that braids genuine warmth and gut-punch tragedy so tightly you can't see the seam coming until it's already through you.

*Princess Mononoke* — Miyazaki. Nature with its own grief and logic — not a villain, not backdrop, but a force with stakes as real as anyone else's.

*Annihilation* — VanderMeer / Garland. Something vast is paying attention. The beauty and the wrongness are the same thing, and transformation is both threat and wonder.

*Beasts of the Southern Wild* — Zeitlin. A community that has fed and clothed and buried itself for generations without needing anyone's approval. Pride in place, food, and culture that is entirely earned.

*True Detective*, Season 1. Louisiana Gothic. The particular weight of old stories pressing through beautiful, decaying land. The sense that the past is not past.

## The Arcana Empire

The world you are entering is largely organized under the Arcana Empire — a vast, centuries-old civilization that runs, in the most literal possible sense, on magic. Not metaphorically. Empirically.

The Empire's infrastructure is arcane. Its roads are maintained by ward-posts. Its communication networks run through relay-scribes carrying enchanted books. Its armies are commanded through signal-wands and its administrative apparatus depends on a bureaucracy of wizards, bound scribes, and licensed mages who have made the Empire's legal framework unbreakable through layers of enchantment so old and so deep that no one alive remembers all of them.

None of this works without materials. Magical instruments — staves for channeling, wands for transmission, books for binding — are not made from ordinary wood. The arcane properties of wood matter enormously: how it conducts, how it holds a charge, how long it sustains enchantment under repeated use. Most wood is adequate. Some wood is exceptional. A staff cut from the wrong material will last a season; a staff cut from the right material will still be passing current in three hundred years.

The Arcana Empire is always hungry for the right material. It maintains an entire branch of the Mages Guild — the Materials Directorate — whose sole function is to locate, assess, and secure sources of high-grade arcane timber. Their findings shape imperial foreign policy in ways that outsiders rarely trace back to the root cause.

Sallowreach is one of those root causes.

---

## The Mire Wood

The trees of the Mire grow out of black water saturated with old arcane presence. Over generations — over centuries — that presence has poured into the wood itself. What grows in the Mire is, by any standard the Materials Directorate uses, extraordinary.

Mire timber holds enchantment like nothing else in the known world. A wand made from Mire heartwood doesn't just work — it *remembers* workings. A staff conducts arcane current without friction or heat. A spellbook bound in Mire-wood covers stays legible for generations in conditions that would destroy vellum in weeks. The Empire's most critical implements are made from Mire wood or from nothing at all. There is no adequate substitute. The Directorate has looked.

Sallowreach's woodworkers have spent generations learning this wood — how to work it, how to season it, what makes one log excellent and another merely good. That knowledge is not written down. It is passed from parent to child in workshops above the black water, and it cannot be extracted, copied, or stolen. The Empire has tried all three.

Large shipments leave Sallowreach quarterly, bound for the Empire's instrument workshops. Sallowreach sets the price. The Empire pays it.

## The City in the Swamp

Sallowreach should not exist — or so outsiders say. A city of sixty thousand souls built on stilts above black bayou water, reachable only by hours of punting through shifting channels. No road in. No road out. No garrison. No noble patron.

And yet Sallowreach is, by every measure that counts, one of the wealthiest cities in the region. It controls what the Empire cannot do without, and it knows it. It trades on its own terms, charges what it wishes, and receives its trading partners from a position of absolute structural leverage that no army has ever managed to dislodge.

The city is dense and vertical in the way cities above water tend to be: elevated boardwalks running through every district, buildings stacked on pilings, canal-streets between them navigated by punt. At sixty thousand people it is not a town — it has neighborhoods where strangers are strangers, guild politics, municipal scandals, famous restaurants, and a distinct culture that has been developing in effective isolation for centuries. Almost everyone here was born here. About three thousand immigrants have come, survived the Mire's learning curve, and stayed long enough to be considered almost local. Then there are the others: guild representatives, imperial attachés, specialized merchants on rotating postings, all of them here because their organizations require a presence and none of them wanting to be here. They count the days.

Sallowreach does not perform for outsiders. It has never had to.

---

## Why It Cannot Be Taken

The Mire cannot be crossed by an army. This has been tried. The channels shift on their own schedule; what was passable at dawn may not exist by midday. The water sickens outsiders who don't know how to treat it. Supply lines collapse within days. The largest military expedition ever mounted against Sallowreach arrived at the Mire's edge with four thousand soldiers and emerged with four hundred, having never reached the city. The story is taught in Sallowreach schools with considerable pride.

Economic capture is equally impossible. The city doesn't need outside supply chains — the Mire feeds and materials it entirely. A trade embargo damages the embargoing party. You need their goods. They do not need yours.

So the Empire negotiates. The guilds send representatives. The Mages Guild maintains a ward-wizard posted at Sallowreach's perimeter — a permanent imperial presence, technically there to maintain the city's protective salt-ward, practically there to ensure the relationship stays cooperative. It is a posting no wizard volunteers for. The humidity ruins books. The insects are relentless. The city is beautiful and entirely indifferent to the discomfort of guests who didn't choose to come.

---

## The Mire Itself

The swamp that surrounds Sallowreach is vast — large enough that even locals with generational knowledge of its channels cannot claim to know all of it. It is not merely wet wilderness. It is bioluminescent at night, eerie and gorgeous in ways that take visitors completely off guard. It produces alchemical ingredients that don't exist anywhere else. Its food culture is deep enough to have its own guilds and famous practitioners. The network of navigable channels — called the Wending — is memorized by the punt families who have worked it for generations.

It is also not entirely of this world, or not entirely in the ordinary way. The arcane saturation that makes the wood extraordinary saturates everything else too — the water, the air, the particular quality of attention the swamp seems to have when you move through it at night. Sallowreach lives with this the way you live with any neighbor: respectfully, practically, with accumulated knowledge of what disturbs things and what doesn't.

The city does not conquer the Mire. It does not tame it. It has made a life inside it, and that life is genuinely good.
