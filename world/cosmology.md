# Cosmology — The Honored Guests

*GM Eyes Only*

## The Spiritual Life of Sallowreach

The people of Sallowreach are not irreligious. They are deeply, practically spiritual — in the way that people who live above black water in a magical swamp cannot help but be. The bioluminescence moves in patterns that don't follow wind. Offerings left at certain roots disappear. Channels that should be impassable open for people the Mire seems to like. You don't need theology to know you share your home with forces larger than yourself.

Sallowreach's spiritual life doesn't have a name for itself because it doesn't think of itself as a religion. It's closer to an etiquette. A protocol for living alongside presences you can't fully see or understand. There are no priests. No scripture. No formal worship. What there is: an understanding, passed down through families and lived in the body, that the Mire is full of spirits. How many? Unknown — it would be presumptuous to count. What are their names? Unknown — it would be presumptuous to name something that hasn't told you its name.

Sallowreach calls them **honored guests**.

Presences sharing the space. Deserving of welcome and respect, the way any guest in your home deserves welcome and respect. You don't command an honored guest. You don't make demands. You make them comfortable, signal that they are welcome, put on the best table you can. If they choose to make themselves known, that is a gift. If they don't, that is their right. You behave well regardless.

The Mire is the house. The city is the host. The spirits are guests who have been welcome here longer than the city has existed.

**The horrible irony:** one of the honored guests cannot leave.

### The Spirit World & the Veil *(setting metaphysics — Worlds Beyond Number–flavored)*

Behind the etiquette is a literal cosmology the city only half-articulates. There is a **spirit world** alongside this one, divided from it by a **veil** — thick most places, **thin in the Mire,** worn to gauze in the deep channels. Spirits cross. They always have, and they come in every shape, size, and degree of power, from things that could drown a city to things barely able to keep a candle lit.

Three principles the GM should hold:
- **Spirits are not good or evil by nature.** A spirit is a *person* from somewhere else — wants, temperament, kindness and cruelty in the same human proportions. The "honored guests" framing is exactly right: you treat a guest with care because you don't know them, not because they're holy.
- **Small spirits can pass as human.** A spirit of modest power can put on a human seeming and live an ordinary life — usually not as a plot, but out of curiosity, loneliness, or love of something here. Most people never know they've met one. *(See [[Vael Miroux]], who can* see *which neighbors aren't human, and the "Old Basile" quest — the clean, in-town showcase of this.)*
- **Spirits collect titles, not names.** Nothing from across the veil carries a name the way people do — it *accumulates* what each era and community calls it, layered like sediment. A small spirit may hold exactly one, worn like a favorite coat (Basile). Something old and powerful wears half a dozen, and the count is legible power: **count the titles and you've weighed the spirit.** [[Vael Miroux]] teaches it exactly that bluntly. The vastest things — the [[Mangrove Spirit]], the [[Tree Spirit]] — have titles the way a coastline has names; no one has ever counted them. *(Worked example: [[the-tallykeeper|the Tallykeeper]], six titles deep. [[old-patience|Old Patience]] holds two or three, which should now feel exactly right.)*

This is **player-facing metaphysics,** not a secret — bloodhunters, [[Delphine]], the whole honored-guest tradition all assume it. What stays gated is the campaign's specific truth: that the vastest "spirit" of all, the [[Tree Spirit]], didn't *cross* — it was *bound,* deliberately, by the city, and the Celebration is the binding. The general spirit-world cosmology is the *water* the secret swims in; reveal the water freely, guard the fish. (The party's own conditions are spirit-world problems: the tether is a binding; [[Isotta]]'s ghost-curse is a human *tied* in a spirit's shape, not a natural crossing.)

## The Spirits

### The Mangrove Spirit

An ancient spirit of the Mire. Free, vast, and diffuse — it does not think in human terms. No concept of towns, people, or property. The swamp's growth is not malicious. It's the spirit reaching, spreading, searching. The expansion is longing. The spirit barely notices [[Sallowreach]] exists. The city is a splinter in its body, an irritation it doesn't register.

The Mangrove Spirit moves in geological time. It does not hurry. It will still be searching long after the party is gone, if nothing changes.

### The Tree Spirit

The Mangrove Spirit's companion. Husband/wife energy — two halves of the same ecosystem, each incomplete without the other. Trapped for generations by the binding woven into Sallowreach's weekly [[The Celebration|Celebration]].

The Tree Spirit's essence has nowhere to go. It pours into the trees — which is why [[Sallowreach#Why Sallowreach Is Rich|Mire lumber]] is extraordinary, dense and beautiful in ways no other forest produces. It may also leach into the water, which is worth considering as the reason [[The Tether|the black water is charged]]. Its nature saturates everything it touches.

The Tree Spirit was trapped by Sallowreach's founders, deliberately, long ago. What began as a calculated act has become invisible tradition. Nobody alive remembers it as imprisonment.

### Their Relationship

Companion spirits. The Mangrove Spirit's endless growth is grief — it is searching for the Tree Spirit. The campaign's emotional core is a love story. Two beings separated by an act a city has forgotten it committed.

The party will not understand this at first. The Mire's growth reads as threat. The Mangrove Spirit reads as indifferent or hostile. The reveal that the expansion is *longing* is one of the campaign's key emotional reframes.

## The Turn & The Tether

*The mechanism behind the party's curse — and the campaign's central recognition reveal. Guard the timing of what they can know.*

The [[Tree Spirit]]'s essence saturates all the Mire's black water; it is charged everywhere, always. But the binding is **acoustic** — the old chants, the drum pattern, the torch positions — and once a week, at the [[The Celebration|Celebration]], it is *performed at full power.* On Celebration nights the binding peaks and the whole Mire's water **Turns.**

**The Turn** — the binding singing, made physical in the water:
- the black water goes *glass-still* for a few breaths;
- a single slow pulse of light swells up from far beneath — deeper and more sustained than the [[lantern-rays|rays]]' traveling shimmer, rising and fading like a held breath;
- the air tastes of iron and crushed green stems;
- anything *in* the water feels a low pressure — a vast hand slowly closing.

It happens Mire-wide, not just in the city: the city sees it as the canals lighting beneath the released lanterns; far out in the channels it passes unseen by anyone but the fish. **The party's ship went down, far from Sallowreach, during a Turn.** They drowned in the binding at the height of its song and came up *tethered* — caught in the same net that holds the Tree Spirit. That is why their curse is so complete, and why it took *them* and not amphibious [[Remy Duval|Remy]], who worked the surface and never went fully under.

**They are a small binding.** This is the campaign's quiet engine: the party's own condition is a working model of the Tree Spirit's. Investigating their curse leads, by its own logic, toward the truth about the Celebration — and at the end the gut-punch lands because they recognize the spirit's imprisonment as *their curse writ enormous:* "we've been stuck for weeks and it's unbearable; it has been stuck for two hundred years."

**The cure is downstream of the binding.** Because they're caught in the same weave, **resolving the binding lifts their tether** — which entangles the personal stake with the central choice: freeing the Tree Spirit frees *them,* at the cost of the city. (And a partial fix that lifts the tether *without* freeing the spirit — see [[resolution-options]] — lets them walk free and leave the injustice in place. That is a knife.)

### The Recognition Reveal — what they can know, and when

They were too far away to connect a curse in open water to a ritual they hadn't seen, so they don't *deduce* it — they **recognize** it. Split it in two:

- **The mid-game click** *(earnable):* at a Celebration — the **second** one, once they're primed and positioned by the water — they witness The Turn and feel their tether *answer* it. *"Our curse and this ritual are the same force."* They now suspect the Celebration's magic took them. They do **not** yet know it is a prison.
- **The end-game gut-punch** *(still hidden):* that the Turn is a *binding,* that it holds a *Tree Spirit,* that the growth is its companion's *grief,* that the founders did it on purpose and the city forgot. These land later, through the chants (Vane's journal → [[Caspar Renne]] → the [[the-tidebreaker|spear]]).

**Never let an NPC say "the Celebration cursed you."** It is a thread they pull. The clue chain that feeds the click lives in [[session-01-prep]]; the recognition beat in [[The Celebration]].

## The Ward & The Empire

*This section is GM-only. The party can piece it together over Acts 1–2.*

Two hundred years ago, the Mages Guild noticed something anomalous in the Mire: a major spirit moving with consistent directionality toward Sallowreach. Not random Mire growth — intentional approach. The Guild sent a practitioner to investigate and, finding no quick answer, negotiated a permanent posting. The arrangement has persisted ever since.

**What the Empire knows:** The Mangrove Spirit is being drawn toward Sallowreach. Salt disrupts its approach. The ward holds it back. They do not know why the spirit is drawn there.

**What the Empire does not know:** That the Tree Spirit is trapped inside the city. That the Celebration is a binding ritual. That what the Mangrove Spirit is "searching for" is its imprisoned companion. The Guild has been maintaining a ward against grief without knowing what the grief is.

**The economic arrangement:** Sallowreach pays for the posting in lumber — quarterly shipments of Mire wood to Guild operations in the capital. Both parties understand this as tribute. Sallowreach pays for protection it requires. The Guild provides the service for which it is paid. Neither party pretends otherwise. The timber Sallowreach provides is extraordinary (GM SECRET: because of the Tree Spirit's saturating essence), which makes the arrangement economically sensible from the Guild's side regardless of the spiritual mechanics.

**What the current wizard knows:** Technically, very little. They know the ward holds back Mire growth and that the spirit moves "directionally" rather than randomly — this is in the institutional records somewhere. They have not read the records carefully. They have been too consumed by their own crisis to do the research.

**The first maintainer's records:** [[The First Maintainer's Journal|Aldric Vane's field journal]] documents eleven years of observation. He identified the directional pull, confirmed the attractor was internal to Sallowreach rather than environmental, and could not identify the source. He left the question for his successors. No successor has answered it. The journal's current location is tracked in [[Caspar Renne]]'s research files — it went to an estate auction six years ago and was lost to him.

**The irony the Empire lives in:** They have maintained a ward against the Mangrove Spirit's approach for two hundred years, taking excellent lumber as payment, and the thing drawing the spirit there is the very binding that makes Sallowreach's lumber extraordinary. The arrangement is self-perpetuating in ways no one has noticed.

## The Celebration: The Truth

*This section contains the campaign's central GM secret. Guard it.*

The weekly [[The Celebration|Celebration]] is genuine — the devotion is real, the spiritual meaning is sincere, the city means every word of every chant. It is also a binding ritual.

The original purpose has been lost to time. What began as deliberate imprisonment became invisible tradition, then cultural identity, then sincere spiritual practice spanning generations. The chanting keeps the Tree Spirit trapped. The specific elements that "welcome" the honored guests — the drum patterns, the positioning of the torches, the words of the oldest chants — are, unknowingly, the mechanism of imprisonment.

The people of Sallowreach have been faithfully, lovingly hosting a prisoner for generations and calling it a welcome.

They would be devastated to learn this. They are not complicit in malice. They are complicit in ignorance — which is different, and in some ways harder to resolve, because it means the people doing harm are the same people the party has come to love.

**The GM's best scene:** the moment the players understand what the Celebration actually does, and then have to attend it anyway, watching sixty thousand people celebrate in genuine joy and devotion. Let that breathe.

### What Stopping the Celebration Would Do

*This is the mechanism, not a prediction. Whether anyone stops the Celebration — and whether the party even wants to — is entirely open. What follows is what *would* happen if the binding ends, so the GM knows what's at stake whichever way the table moves.*

If the Celebration stops, the binding breaks. The Tree Spirit is freed. The Mangrove Spirit finds its companion. The two reunite — and the Mire's growth stops, because the longing ends.

The cost, if it comes to that:
- The Celebration itself — gone, or irrevocably changed. The city's identity is built around it.
- The extraordinary lumber — gone. The Tree Spirit's essence no longer pours into the trees. The wood becomes ordinary. Sallowreach's most valuable export vanishes.
- The ward — may destabilize. [[The Wizard]]'s salt-based ward was built in relationship to the binding. What happens when the binding ends is not fully predictable. [VERIFY: worth deciding before Act 3]

The party has to weigh: their freedom, the spirits' reunion, and the end of a beautiful wrong — against the cost to sixty thousand people who never chose any of this.
