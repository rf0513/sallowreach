# Sallowreach — Campaign Bible (GM Eyes Only)

## Overview

A 6-10 session Daggerheart campaign for 4 players, Tier 1 (possibly touching Tier 2 by the end). This is a tight sandbox set in and around a single bayou settlement. Personal stakes, not world-saving stakes. First Daggerheart campaign for the group — designed to be self-contained with a natural endpoint that can open into a longer campaign if the table wants to continue.

## Touchstones & Tone

- **Tone:** Adventure with emotional gut-punches. Brennan Lee Mulligan's sweet spot — moments of genuine warmth, humor, and community that make the hard moments hit harder.
- **Inspirations:** The Wizard, The Witch, and The Wild One (Brennan Lee Mulligan), Princess Mononoke (nature as force, not villain), Southern Gothic atmosphere, Annihilation (the Mire's alien beauty)
- **What this is NOT:** Horror. Grimdark. "Poor sad backwater town." The town is vibrant and proud. The swamp is eerie but wondrous, not just scary.

## The Setting: Sallowreach

A city of roughly 60,000 souls, built on stilts above the black water of the Mire. Not a town that grew large — a city that was always going to be a city, because what the Mire produces demands one. Think fantasy New Orleans crossed with Venice: a dense maze of elevated boardwalks, stilted buildings, canal-streets navigated by punt, neighborhoods stacked on neighborhoods, all of it over dark water.

**Population breakdown:**
- ~95% native-born Sallowreachers, multigenerational, deeply rooted
- ~3,000 immigrants who arrived, survived the Mire's learning curve, and stayed — now considered "almost local" after a decade or two
- Several hundred guild representatives, imperial trade attachés, and specialized merchants on rotating postings — they live in designated quarters, hate the humidity, and count the days until rotation

At this scale, not everyone knows everyone. There are neighborhoods where strangers are strangers. There is local politics, faction tension between old families and new money, guild infighting, and at least one ongoing municipal scandal the players can stumble into. The city has its own bureaucracy, its own courts, its own prison (a stilted structure over particularly deep water, locally called the Soak).

### Why Sallowreach Is Rich

- **Rare alchemical ingredients.** The Mire grows mosses, fungi, roots, venoms, and bioluminescent algae that don't exist anywhere else. Healers, alchemists, and mages across the region pay serious money for Sallowreach exports. The Alchemists' Consortium has a permanent presence here — their representatives among the most miserable of the posting-haters, since their work requires them to actually go into the Mire regularly.
- **Extraordinary lumber.** The wood from Mire trees is prized — dense, waterproof, beautiful grain. Sallowreach woodworkers and boat builders are the best in the region. Generations of craft knowledge that can't be replicated. The Shipwrights' Guild has been trying to steal the techniques for thirty years. **(GM SECRET: The wood is extraordinary because the trapped Tree Spirit pours its essence into the trees with nowhere else to go.)**
- **Unique food culture.** The bayou feeds its people extraordinarily well — crawfish, catfish, wild rice, rare spices that only grow in brackish water. The culinary tradition runs deep enough to have its own professional guilds, famous chefs, and restaurants that attract wealthy visitors who suffer the journey specifically to eat. The Empire's court has been trying to import Sallowreach cooks for generations; the cooks mostly refuse.
- **Skilled artisans.** Weavers using reed and moss fibers, leatherworkers using swamp creature hides, boat builders with unmatched technique. There are entire districts organized around specific crafts.
- **The Punt Network.** Sallowreach controls transit through the Mire. Every merchant, every guild shipment, every imperial attaché gets in and out via Sallowreach's licensed punt operators. The city taxes this. Generously.

### Why It's Not Conquered

Every empire that has looked at Sallowreach's export ledger has wanted to own it. None have managed it. The reasons are structural:

The Mire cannot be crossed by an army. It has been tried. The channels shift. The water sickens outsiders who don't know how to treat it. Supply lines collapse within days. The largest military expedition ever mounted against Sallowreach arrived at the Mire's edge with 4,000 soldiers and emerged with 400, having never reached the city. The story is taught in Sallowreach schools with considerable pride.

Economic capture is equally impossible. The city doesn't need outside supply chains — the Mire feeds and materials it entirely. A trade embargo hurts the embargoing party more than Sallowreach. You need their goods. They don't need yours.

So instead, the Empire trades. The guilds negotiate. They send representatives to maintain relationships and file reports and quietly lobby for favorable terms — and Sallowreach listens politely, charges what it wants, and reminds everyone, gently, that the alternative is nothing.

The guild and imperial representatives know exactly what they are: supplicants with nice titles. Most of them find this humiliating. A few, after enough years in the city, stop finding it humiliating and stop wanting to go home. Those are the ones who eventually become immigrants.

### Spiritual Life: The Honored Guests

The people of Sallowreach are not irreligious. They are deeply, practically spiritual — in the way that people who live above black water in a magical swamp cannot help but be. The bioluminescence moves in patterns that don't follow wind. Offerings left at certain roots disappear. Channels that should be impassable open for people the Mire seems to like. You don't need theology to know you share your home with forces larger than yourself.

Sallowreach's spiritual life doesn't have a name for itself because it doesn't think of itself as a religion. It's closer to an etiquette. A protocol for living alongside presences you can't fully see or understand. There are no priests. No scripture. No formal worship. What there is: an understanding, passed down through families and lived in the body, that the Mire is full of spirits. How many? Unknown — it would be presumptuous to count. What are their names? Unknown — it would be presumptuous to name something that hasn't told you its name. What does Sallowreach call them?

**Honored guests.**

That's the term. Presences sharing the space. Deserving of welcome and respect, the way any guest in your home deserves welcome and respect. You don't command an honored guest. You don't make demands of them. You make them comfortable, you signal that they are welcome, you put on the best table you can. If they choose to make themselves known, that is a gift. If they don't, that is also their right. You behave well regardless.

This is not passive. Sallowreach takes hospitality to spirits seriously, the same way it takes hospitality to people seriously. The Mire is the house; the city is the host; the spirits are guests who have been welcome here longer than the city has existed. The city's job is to make sure they feel that welcome every week.

**The horrible irony:** one of the honored guests cannot leave.

### The Weekly Celebration

Not a town gathering — a city-wide religious event, though Sallowreach would resist the word "religious." The city prefers "the Celebration." Every week, without fail: music before dawn in the outer districts, building through the day until by evening the whole city is in motion. The boardwalks clear of commerce. Chanting in old words, dancing in patterns that vary by family and district and have been passed down for generations. Feasting from communal tables that stretch the length of major thoroughfares. Canal-streets fill with decorated punt boats carrying musicians. Light-lanterns are released over the black water to drift out toward the Mire — a gesture of welcome, of saying: you are invited, the door is open, come if you will.

The Celebration is the act of hospitality. It is Sallowreach saying, every week, to whatever honored guests share their swamp: *we see you. You are welcome here. This is for you.*

Different districts have their own traditions within the larger whole. Old families have roles passed down for generations — the drum-keepers of the Thornside quarter, the rice-guild's ceremonial serving procession, the Mire-edge hunters' torchlight procession through the outer boardwalks. Newcomers learn the general forms within their first few weeks. The family-specific traditions take years. Some families claim their roles trace back to the founding.

The guild and imperial representatives are expected to participate, or at least to be visibly present and not visibly contemptuous. Those who refuse entirely don't last long in their postings — Sallowreach remembers, and its memory affects contract terms. A surprising number of posting-haters find that participating in the Celebration is the moment the city begins to get under their skin.

**GM SECRET:** The celebration is a binding ritual. The original purpose has been lost to time — what began as deliberate imprisonment has become invisible tradition, then cultural identity, then sincere spiritual practice. The chanting and dancing keeps the Tree Spirit trapped. The specific elements that "welcome" the honored guests — the drum patterns, the positioning of the torches, the words of the oldest chants — are, unknowingly, the mechanism of imprisonment. The people of Sallowreach have been faithfully, lovingly hosting a prisoner for generations and calling it a welcome.

They would be devastated to learn this. They are not complicit in malice. They are complicit in ignorance — which is different, and in some ways harder to resolve, because it means the people doing harm are the same people the party has come to love.

**The GM's best scene:** the moment the players understand what the Celebration actually does, and then have to attend it anyway, watching sixty thousand people celebrate in genuine joy and devotion. Let that breathe.

## The Mire

The surrounding swamp. Dense mangrove, black water, shifting waterways. Beautiful and dangerous. Bioluminescent at night. Travel is by punt boat through narrow channels that change as water levels shift. Outsiders get lost. Locals know the ways but even they avoid the deep Mire.

**The Mire is growing.** Slowly but noticeably. Boardwalks that were clear last season are now choked with roots. Fishing channels are narrowing. The city's outer edges are being swallowed inch by inch.

## The Spirits

### The Mangrove Spirit (The Swamp)

An ancient spirit of the Mire. Free, vast, and diffuse. Does not think in human terms — no concept of towns, people, property. The swamp's growth is not malicious. It's the spirit reaching, spreading, searching for its companion. The expansion is longing. The spirit barely notices Sallowreach exists. The town is a splinter in its body, an irritation it doesn't register.

### The Tree Spirit (The Wood)

The Mangrove Spirit's companion. Husband/wife energy — two halves of the same ecosystem. Trapped for generations by the binding woven into Sallowreach's weekly celebration. The extraordinary quality of the Mire's wood is a byproduct — the spirit's essence pours into the trees because it has nowhere else to go.

The Tree Spirit was trapped by Sallowreach's founders, long ago. What began as a deliberate act has become invisible tradition. Nobody alive remembers it as imprisonment.

### Their Relationship

They are companion spirits. The Mangrove Spirit's growing is grief — it's searching for the Tree Spirit. The campaign's emotional core is a love story. Two beings separated by an act the town has forgotten it committed.

## The McGuffin: The Fishing Spear

A fishing spear carved by the Tree Spirit from its own wood as a gift for the Mangrove Spirit. Carved from a single piece of wood with grain colors and patterns no living tree produces — the wood shifts between amber, deep green, and near-black depending on the angle of light, which no Sallowreach woodworker can explain or replicate. To anyone without context it looks like a beautifully crafted but ordinary fishing spear: nice balance, impressive craftsmanship, clearly handmade. An artifact collector's curiosity. Which is exactly how the mafioso thinks of it.

A player will probably want to use it as a weapon. Let them. Let them get attached. Then make them give it up.

The spear is the key to reaching the spirits — not a weapon that lifts the tether on its own, but the thing that makes communication possible. The spirits lifting the tether is a consequence of the party completing the actual work: reuniting them, or freeing the Tree Spirit, or whatever the resolution looks like. It was never meant to be powerful. It was meant to be personal.

### Current Location: Marcel Dray's Collection

The spear is owned by Marcel Dray — acquired as settlement on a debt from the estate of a dead collector who didn't know what he had either. It sits displayed in his private rooms as a conversation piece. He finds it genuinely pleasing to look at. He has no idea what it is, and he doesn't particularly care.

Marcel will not sell it. Not because he values it, but because he already knows exactly what he wants for it: the wizard's disgrace. Not death — he doesn't need to say that part. Just fired, credentials revoked, Mages Guild protection permanently removed.

**The deal:** Marcel has detailed evidence of the wizard's embezzlement — enough to end his career and his guild standing. He can't use it himself without revealing he's been monitoring a guild wizard's finances, which invites scrutiny he doesn't want. The party, desperate outsiders with no obvious connection to his operation, are the clean instrument he's been waiting for. His offer is straightforward: *deliver the embezzlement evidence to the Mages Guild in a way that results in the wizard's removal, and the spear is yours.*

**What Marcel doesn't volunteer:** Once guild protection is revoked, the wizard's debt becomes immediately collectable. Marcel is not cruel about this — it's simply the conclusion of a business arrangement. He will confirm it plainly if asked directly. The party has to think to ask.

**See full Marcel Dray section for how to run this character.**

### How the Party Learns About the Spear

The party follows a chain. Nobody hands them the answer — each NPC knows one piece.

**Step 1 — The Healer**
The healer is the natural first stop: the party is cursed, the healer is the person in Sallowreach who deals with things that have gone wrong with bodies and magic. The healer examines the tether, probably through some combination of mundane medicine and Mire-spiritual intuition. Their diagnosis:

- The tether is spirit-water magic — the black water charged them with something ancient, and now they're bound to it
- The healer has no cure. This is outside their practice.
- But they know the shape of the problem: *"You need something that speaks the spirit's language back at it. Not a spell. Not medicine. Something that was made by the Mire, for the Mire."*
- They point the party toward the Old Stories Keeper — the one person in the city who actually studies what the Mire has made and why.

The healer believes what they're saying. They're not certain it will work. They are certain it's the party's best lead.

**Step 2 — The Old Stories Keeper**
This NPC has spent decades collecting oral traditions, old texts, and Mire-lore that most of Sallowreach considers quaint. They know about the spirits — more than they can prove, less than they wish. When the party describes what the healer told them, the Old Stories Keeper gets very still.

What they know:
- The Tree Spirit exists. The old accounts describe it clearly — a spirit of wood and heartwood, companion to the Mangrove Spirit, last recorded freely present in the Mire many generations ago.
- The Tree Spirit was a maker. The accounts describe gifts it carved from itself — objects that carried the spirit's own nature, able to cross between the spirit world and the physical.
- One such object is described in detail: a fishing spear, carved as a gift that was never delivered. The accounts say it has impossible wood — colors no living tree produces.
- The Old Stories Keeper has been trying to find it for years. They tracked it to an estate sale. They tracked it from the estate sale to its buyer.

*"It's sitting in [mafioso's name]'s private collection. I've asked about it twice. I wasn't taken seriously either time."*

What the Old Stories Keeper cannot guarantee: that the spear will lift the tether. What they believe: that the spear is the closest thing to a bridge between the party and the spirits that exists in the physical world. If anything can open that conversation, it's this.

The party now has a destination. The problem is who's at it.

### GM Notes on the Information Chain

- The healer should feel like a dead end that opens a door, not a quest-giver. They don't say "go find the spear." They say "I can't help you, but maybe she can."
- The Old Stories Keeper should feel like someone who's been waiting years for people who would actually listen. This conversation matters to them personally. Let that show.
- Neither NPC knows the full story. The healer doesn't know about the Tree Spirit. The Old Stories Keeper doesn't know the spear will definitely work — they believe it, but belief isn't certainty.
- The wizard knows all of this and has said nothing. When the party eventually finds this out, it recontextualizes every interaction they've had with the wizard.

## The Wizard

Appointed by the Mages Guild to maintain a perpetual ward (salt-based magic) that holds back the Mire's growth. A middling bureaucrat who drew a bad posting and has been here too long. They understand the ward technically — what it does, how to maintain it, when it needs reinforcement — but they have no particular insight into why the Mire grows, what the spirits are, or what the Celebration actually does. They do their job. Mostly.

The problem is the gambling.

The wizard has a serious gambling problem, has had it for years, and Sallowreach's underground card rooms — which are exclusively the mafioso's operation — have been very happy to accommodate them. The debt is enormous. In any other context, a debt like this gets you killed. But the wizard carries Mages Guild credentials, and the mafioso has a rule: you don't kill guild members. The political blowback isn't worth it.

So instead the mafioso found another kind of leverage. Somewhere along the way the wizard started skimming — embezzling from the guild funds allocated for ward maintenance, siphoning "research expenses" that don't exist. The mafioso found out. Now they have the debt AND the evidence of embezzlement, and the wizard is trapped: they can't leave the posting without the debt following them, can't clear the debt, and can't afford for the guild to find out what they've done.

**What this means for the ward:** The embezzlement has consequences. The wizard is underfunding maintenance. The ward is holding, but not as well as it should be. The Mire's accelerating growth isn't entirely the spirits' doing — some of it is a poorly maintained ward run by a distracted, exhausted, cornered person who's been skimming the budget.

**What the wizard knows:** Technical knowledge of the ward. Institutional memory of the posting — records going back to their predecessors, which a thorough party could find useful. Surface-level knowledge of the spirits from those records (they exist, the old postings mention them, the ward was built partly in response to their presence). They have NOT done the deep research the Old Stories Keeper has done. They don't know about the binding or the Celebration's true function. They're not hiding ancient secrets — they just haven't looked.

**What the wizard wants:** Out. Of the debt, of the posting, of the whole situation. They are not a bad person. They are a weak person who made bad choices and then made worse ones to cover the first ones, and now they're in very deep. If the party treats them with any kindness, the wizard will respond to it — which makes what's coming harder.

**The mafioso's play:** The mafioso cannot expose the embezzlement himself without revealing he's been monitoring a guild wizard's finances — which would invite guild scrutiny into his operations. He needs the evidence to come from somewhere clean. The party, being desperate outsiders with no obvious ties to him, are exactly what he needs. The deal he'll offer them: *get me the wizard's head — not literally, just fired, disgraced, guild protection revoked — and I'll give you the spear.*

What the mafioso does not tell them: once the guild pulls protection, the debt comes due immediately. The wizard knows this. If the party ever tells the wizard what the mafioso is asking them to do, the wizard will beg. And mean it.

## Marcel Dray

The man who runs Sallowreach's underground economy has been in the city for eleven years. He arrived with nothing — or close to nothing, and what he had was the kind of nothing that comes after losing everything else. He built his operation from a single card table in the back of a distillery. Now he owns the distillery, three more like it, and the card tables are in a private room that the city's wealthiest residents pay for the privilege of losing money in.

His name is Marcel Dray. He is, by any reasonable accounting, dead.

### The Execution

The Empire executed him twelve years ago for crimes they've since classified. The method was official and thorough — the Empire takes its executions seriously, particularly for people who've embarrassed them. He was pronounced dead by the attending physician, a Guild-certified practitioner with no reason to lie. He was interred. He did not stay interred.

What happened in between is something Marcel doesn't discuss. Not from trauma — he simply finds the question uninteresting. He died. He came back. He came to Sallowreach because it's as far from imperial jurisdiction as you can get without leaving the continent, and because legally he's already dead, which makes extradition complicated. He has been here ever since.

### The Aesthetic

Marcel Dray is tall, unhurried, and dressed in clothes whose quality is immediately apparent without being flashy. He has excellent manners. He listens more than he speaks. When he does speak, it's worth paying attention to, not because he's theatrical about it — he isn't — but because he doesn't say things he doesn't mean.

His hands are cold. Not cool — cold. The kind of cold that doesn't belong to a living body. When he shakes your hand, you feel it immediately, and the cold spreads up your wrist before he lets go. He doesn't apologize for it. He doesn't explain it. In eleven years, no one in Sallowreach has asked him about it to his face.

He doesn't sweat. In the bayou humidity, where everyone is always damp, Marcel Dray is always dry. His breath doesn't fog in cold air. Food and drink pass his lips at social occasions; whether he requires them is unclear and he's not going to tell you. He moves through the world like a man who has already settled all his accounts with it.

When something displeases him, he goes very still and very quiet. This is more frightening than anger. No one who has been on the receiving end of Marcel Dray's stillness has failed to understand what it meant.

### The Operation

**The Pale Room** — his underground casino, invitation only, the best card games in the city. The room itself is immaculate: white tablecloths, low light, exceptional service. The games are fair. Marcel runs fair games because rigged games create enemies and fair games create addicts, and addicts are more profitable. The wizard has lost a significant sum here over three years.

**The Mire Spirits** — bootleg distillation using Mire ingredients that can't legally be exported. The resulting liquors are extraordinary — nothing like them exists outside Sallowreach, and the markup reflects that. Distributed throughout the city, exported quietly through sympathetic punt operators.

**Information** — his real operation. Everything that happens in Sallowreach eventually reaches Marcel. Guild negotiations, political appointments, who owes whom what. He doesn't sell information cheaply, but he doesn't hoard it either. Information is only valuable in motion.

**The Courtesy** — businesses in certain districts pay a monthly sum for which nothing bad happens to them. The courtesy is reasonable, the protection is real, and Marcel has never had to demonstrate what happens when it lapses, because no one has been foolish enough to find out.

**What he doesn't do:** traffic in people. This is his line, and it is absolute. It's one of the things that makes the city's relationship with him complicated — he's a criminal, but he has a code, and the code has teeth.

### His Relationship to Sallowreach

Marcel Dray has been here eleven years. He participates in the Celebration every week, always near the back, always watching. He contributes to the communal feast tables — generously, anonymously. He has genuine feelings about this city. He is not leaving.

He is also not confused about what he is or what he does. He finds people who moralize at him tiresome. He finds people who are honest about wanting something refreshing.

### The Wizard Problem

The wizard is Marcel's most persistent irritation. The debt is large enough to matter; the guild protection makes collection impossible through normal means; and the embezzlement evidence he's sitting on is useless as long as the guild considers the wizard a protected member in good standing. He needs the wizard disgraced, not just caught — fired, credentials revoked, the guild washing their hands of him publicly. Then the debt comes due.

He has been waiting for the right instrument. The tethered party, desperate and motivated and clean of any obvious connection to his operation, are exactly that.

### Running Marcel

- He treats the party with complete respect and total honesty — within the limits of what they ask. He doesn't volunteer the fact that getting the wizard fired means the wizard dies. He will confirm it, plainly, if asked. He considers it the party's responsibility to ask the right questions.
- He honors deals. This is non-negotiable — his reputation depends on it.
- He is not the campaign's villain. He's a complicating force with his own logic. The party may end up liking him. That's fine. It doesn't change what he is.
- If the party tries to steal the spear rather than deal for it, Marcel will know within hours, and he will be disappointed rather than angry. Disappointed is worse.

## The Curse: The Tether

### What Happened

Session one opens with a shipwreck. The party were passengers on the same vessel — strangers, not traveling together, each heading somewhere else for their own reasons. The ship broke apart in the Mire. They went under. The black water of the swamp is magically charged, and people who go under in it come out changed.

They wake on a muddy bank at the Mire's edge. Alive. Soaked. Strangers to each other, except for the thing they now share.

### The Tether

The curse is a bond — to each other, and to Sallowreach. Two components:

**Tethered to each other:** The party cannot separate beyond a certain distance without physical resistance setting in — a pull in the chest, directional, like something has a line on them. Push past it and it becomes pain. They cannot meaningfully operate independently. They cannot split the party. They are each other's problem until this is resolved.

**Tethered to Sallowreach:** They can travel anywhere within the Mire — all channels, all depths, including places locals avoid. What they cannot do is leave the Mire system. Every road that leads out of the swamp ends the same way: resistance, then agony, then they're back where they started. They have tried. Everyone tries once.

There are no physical marks. No visible symptom. The curse is purely relational — it's not in their bodies, it's between them and the world.

### Why They Don't Just Leave

They literally cannot. The tether to Sallowreach is absolute. This is not three soft reasons — it is one hard rule. The cure is somewhere in the Mire, and the Mire won't let them go until they find it.

### The Urgency

The tether itself creates urgency, but consider adding personal pressure during session zero: each PC was traveling somewhere. Someone is expecting them. A debt comes due. A sick parent. A job with a deadline. A ship that won't wait. The tether doesn't care. Sallowreach is where they are, and Sallowreach is where they stay.

### Why the Water Is Charged

**GM NOTE — open question to resolve:** The Mire's black water being magically charged is established fact. The *reason* is worth deciding before play begins. Options:

- The water has always been charged — ancient spirit presence saturates the swamp
- The Tree Spirit's trapped essence leaches into the water over generations (mirrors the wood; its nature pours into everything it touches)
- The ward has altered the water chemistry in ways the Mages Guild didn't anticipate or didn't disclose
- Some combination of the above

The third option gives the wizard additional culpability without making them the direct curse-giver. Worth considering.

## Campaign Structure

### Act 1 (Sessions 1-3): Arrival
- Shipwreck in the Mire; wake on the bank as strangers; discover the tether
- Enter Sallowreach, get taken in, navigate being unexpectedly stuck
- Experience the city's warmth, culture, the weekly Celebration
- Hear rumors about the Mire, the spear, things that might help
- Start pulling on NPC threads — information, guides, and leads all come at a price
- Personal urgency: each PC has somewhere they were supposed to be; the tether doesn't care

### Act 2 (Sessions 4-6): The Mire
- Pursue the spear deeper into the swamp
- Encounter the Mangrove Spirit or signs of it
- NPC secrets start unraveling — the wizard's suspicion, the old stories
- Learn about the Tree Spirit (maybe from the wizard under pressure, maybe from Mire encounters)
- The weekly celebration takes on a different meaning

### Act 3 (Sessions 7-8+): The Choice
- The mafioso's deal comes to a head — deliver the embezzlement evidence, get the spear, condemn the wizard
- The party likely realizes (before or after accepting the deal) what revoked guild protection actually means for the wizard
- Obtain the spear — at whatever cost they choose to pay
- Realize it doesn't work on its own — need the spirit(s)
- Confront the truth about the binding and the Celebration
- Final negotiation/confrontation: reunite the spirits? Free the Tree Spirit? What happens to the town?
- The gut-punch: freeing the spirits saves the party but costs Sallowreach its lumber, its Celebration, its identity. The people they've grown to love lose something fundamental.
- Potential second gut-punch, depending on choices: the wizard's fate

## Session Zero Requirements

Each player must privately tell the GM:
1. Where their character was going when they boarded that ship — specifically, not vaguely. What were they traveling toward?
2. Why it matters that they get there. What happens if they don't make it?
3. Who knows they were on that ship — who is expecting them somewhere and will eventually notice they haven't arrived
4. At least one NPC relationship outside of Sallowreach (family, mentor, employer, rival — gives ongoing hooks and pressure)

## Key Locations (To Be Built Out)

- **Sallowreach** — the stilted town itself
- **The Wending** — the navigable waterways locals use, by punt boat
- **The Drowning Garden** — a sunken ruin deeper in the Mire (potential spear location)
- **The Oldstone** — a shrine or landmark the locals avoid (possible original binding site)
- **The Deep Mire** — where the Mangrove Spirit's presence is strongest

## NPCs (To Be Built Out)

Need 10-15 interconnected NPCs. Key roles to fill:
- **The Wizard** — ward maintainer; middling bureaucrat in a bad posting; serious gambling debt to the mafioso; embezzled guild funds to cover losses; trapped by the mafioso's leverage; not a villain, just a weak person in very deep; ward is undermaintained because of the embezzlement; knows surface-level lore but hasn't done the research; will respond to kindness, which makes the mafioso's deal harder
- **A city leader or elder** — represents community interests; deeply invested in the Celebration and what Sallowreach stands for
- **A guide/boatman** — the party's primary way into the Mire; knows the Wending better than anyone; has their own reasons for taking the job
- **The Healer** — first stop for the tethered party; can't cure the tether but correctly identifies it as spirit-water magic and points the party toward the Old Stories Keeper; acts as the party's medical and spiritual resource throughout
- **The Old Stories Keeper** — knows the legend of the Tree Spirit and the undelivered spear; has been tracking the spear for years; knows it's in the mafioso's collection; personally invested in seeing the old accounts proven right
- **Marcel Dray** — head of Sallowreach's underground economy; subtly undead (hands ice cold, doesn't sweat, was executed by the Empire twelve years ago); owns the spear as a decorative curiosity; lawful evil, operates by a strict code, honors deals; his price for the spear is the wizard's disgrace and removal from guild protection; see full section
- **A woodworker/artisan** — master of the trade; connection to the lumber that makes Sallowreach rich; unknowingly connected to the Tree Spirit through their craft; a good person to show the spear to for a reaction
- **A merchant/outsider** — one of the guild or imperial representatives; different perspective on the city; useful for information about the outside world and what the party is missing by being stuck
- **A skeptic** — doesn't believe in spirits or the honored guests tradition; provides grounded counterpoint; interesting to have around when the spirits start making themselves known
