# The Fishing Spear

**Type:** Artifact (appears mundane)
**Current Location:** [[Marcel Dray]]'s private quarters, displayed as a curiosity
**Origin:** Carved by [[The Tree Spirit]] as a gift for [[The Mangrove Spirit]]

---

## Description

Carved from a single piece of wood by the Tree Spirit — a gift that was never delivered. The grain shifts between amber, deep green, and near-black depending on the angle of light, in patterns and colors no living tree produces. The balance is exceptional. The craftsmanship is clearly handmade, and clearly beyond anything a human woodworker could explain.

To anyone without context: a beautifully made fishing spear. An artifact collector's curiosity.

A local woodworker shown the spear will say the technique is unrecognizable and the wood is impossible. They will be unsettled in a way they can't articulate.

Marcel Dray finds it genuinely pleasing to look at. He has no idea what it is.

## What It Actually Does

The spear is a powerful magical artifact, and what it does is **break curses.** It carries the [[Tree Spirit]]'s own nature — the same boundary-crossing power that charges the Mire's black water — and in the right hands it can sever the spirit-magic that holds a soul where it shouldn't be.

- **[[Isotta]]'s ghost-condition and [[the-witch|the Witch]]'s clank-curse are within its power.** Once the party has the spear, those curses can be broken — clean, earned, personal payoffs for two PCs who'll have crossed the whole city to get it. *(GM: stage these as weighty scenes, not a quiet stat change.)*
- **The tether is the exception — the final boss of curses.** The party isn't simply *cursed*; they're *caught,* tangled directly in the great binding at the city's heart (see [[Cosmology#The Turn & The Tether|The Turn]]). The spear can't just snip that. Lifting the tether means resolving the binding itself — freeing or reuniting the spirits — which is the finale, and where the cost to Sallowreach lives. ("Taste now, cure at the finale.")

The spear is also, still, **a bridge between worlds** — carved by the Tree Spirit as a gift for the [[The Mangrove Spirit|Mangrove Spirit]], it opens the conversation with the spirits that the central resolution requires, and in the deep Mire it becomes a compass toward the one it was made for (below).

## How the Party Learns About It

The party follows a chain. Nobody hands them the answer — each NPC knows one piece.

### Step 1 — The Healer

The healer is the natural first stop: the party is cursed, the healer is the person in Sallowreach who deals with things gone wrong with bodies and magic. Through mundane medicine and Mire-spiritual intuition, the healer examines the tether. Their diagnosis:

- This is spirit-water magic — the black water charged them with something ancient
- The healer has no cure. This is outside their practice.
- But they know the shape of the problem: *"You need something that speaks the spirit's language back at it. Not a spell. Not medicine. Something that was made by the Mire, for the Mire."*
- They point the party toward [[The Old Stories Keeper]] — the one person in the city who actually studies what the Mire has made and why.

The healer believes what they're saying. They're not certain it will work. They are certain it's the best lead.

### Step 2 — The Old Stories Keeper

[[Caspar Renne]] lights up when the party describes what Delphine told them — that pull-of-genuine-interest stillness, hands stopping mid-motion. He knows the accounts. He talks clearly and passionately about the Tree Spirit, the undelivered gift, what the impossible wood means.

Then they ask where it is.

Caspar becomes a different person: too much movement, eyes finding shelves, qualifiers multiplying. He knows. It is obvious he knows. He will not say. Naming the person who has it — naming their dealings — to strangers he met an hour ago is not something a careful man does in this city. He doesn't know who these people are. He doesn't know where the information goes.

**What the party gets from this conversation:**
- Full knowledge of the spear's nature and significance
- Confirmation it exists physically and is somewhere in Sallowreach
- The clear, unmistakable impression that Caspar knows exactly where it is and is afraid to say so
- Possibly a glimpse of the empty slot in display case seven, if they're paying attention

**What the party does NOT get:** a name. If they push hard enough, they meet the Warden instead.

**Getting the name** requires trust, time, or lateral investigation:
- Build trust with Caspar over Act 1 — help someone he knows, demonstrate discretion, have Delphine vouch for them specifically
- Find the name another way and return to confirm — asking around about who deals in unusual artifacts, following the gap in case seven, pulling threads elsewhere in the city
- Return with enough context about the tether and the spirits that Caspar judges the risk worth taking

**What Caspar still omits even after naming Marcel:** that the spear is there because of him. That truth surfaces separately — see [[Caspar Renne]].

### Step 3 — Marcel Dray

See [[Marcel Dray]] for the full deal. The short version: Marcel will trade the spear for the wizard's disgrace and removal from guild protection. The party has to figure out what that means for the wizard before or after they commit to it.

## After Acquiring the Spear

The party has the spear. For **Isotta and the Witch, this is the moment their curses can finally be broken** — the artifact does what the artifact does. Give them the scenes; they earned them (see their sheets).

But for the **tether** — the thing all four of them share — it stays quiet. No hum, no glow, no instructions. Because the tether isn't a curse the spear can cut; it's the great binding itself, and the spear's role there isn't to *break* it but to *reach* it. They're holding the key to a conversation they don't yet know how to have. The natural next move is back to Caspar.

### Step 4 — Back to Caspar

The natural move — maybe the only move — is to return to Caspar. He's the one who knew what it was. He'll know what to do with it.

When they arrive and he sees it, he goes very still. That particular stillness — hands stopping, breath held — that the party has already learned to read as *genuine interest*. He doesn't reach for it immediately. He says: *"May I?"*

He holds it for a long moment. Turns it over. Looks at the grain the way someone reads a letter.

Then: *"This won't lift what's tying you here. I don't mean it's powerless — gods, look at it — I mean the thing holding you isn't a curse you *cut.* It was made as a gift. A gift needs someone to receive it."* *(He's right about the tether specifically — whatever the spear has already done for Isotta or the Witch, the binding at the city's heart is a different order of problem.)*

He knows where the Mangrove Spirit is — roughly. Southwest Deep Mire. It's been moving toward Sallowreach from that direction for two hundred years, which is in his own records and confirmed by the journal. He'll give the party the best information available: the last documented sighting from the deep-Mire fishing accounts, the direction to travel, the warning that past the Wending the usual navigation stops working. He can get them pointed the right way.

What he cannot do is tell them what happens when the spirit receives it. He has accounts about the spear being made. He has nothing about the delivery. That part was never written down. *"I don't know what it does when it reaches the one it was made for. I know it was meant to reach them. I imagine that's the point."*

**The scene that belongs here, if you can give it to him:** Caspar has spent forty years researching this object. He has held the empty case in the museum every day for six years. Now it's in his hands and he has about thirty seconds with it before the party takes it into the Deep Mire and possibly never comes back. Give him that thirty seconds. He'll hold it a moment too long before he gives it back. He won't comment on this. Neither should you.

### The Spear in the Mire

Once the party is on the water past the Wending, the spear changes. Not dramatically — no light, no sound. But the balance shifts. It wants to be held pointed southwest. The grain — amber, deep green, near-black — moves slowly in the direction of travel when the spear is pointed correctly, and stills when it isn't.

It is a compass seeking its destination.

A party paying attention will notice this within the first hour on the water. A party not paying attention will figure it out when they get turned around and the grain starts to pulse faintly, the way a compass needle quivers.

It will not lead them around obstacles. It will not protect them. It will simply know where it is going, and if they follow it, so will they.

## GM Notes on the Information Chain

- [[Delphine]] should feel like a dead end that opens a door — she can't cure the tether, but she correctly names the shape of the problem
- Caspar should feel like someone who knows everything and is afraid of it — the party leaves frustrated but certain they've found the right person; the gap between "Caspar knows" and "Caspar tells" is where Act 1 social play lives
- The name is a reward for trust or investigation, not a freebie
- When the party finally reaches Marcel, and Marcel mentions that "someone else has been asking about it" — Caspar's evasion retroactively makes complete sense in a new way, and the party should feel the click of it
