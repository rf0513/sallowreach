# Fen

**Real name:** Fenella Bast — goes by Fen; will correct you once and then stop caring
**Role:** Musician, agitator, accidental saboteur
**Location:** [[Sallowreach]] — no fixed venue; plays wherever will have her, which is increasingly not the nice places
**Faction:** Independent (emphatically)
**Tier:** 1

---

## Appearance

Mid-twenties, small and loud about it. She wears color in a city that trends toward the muted — deep reds, cracked yellows, the kind of choices that read as deliberate provocation in Sallowreach's bayou aesthetic. Her hair is cut short on one side and not on the other, which several guild members have described to her face as "unfortunate." Her instrument is a modified frame drum: the head replaced twice, a row of brass rings hammered around the rim that she added herself, the whole thing beaten to a particular resonance she found by accident and has been chasing ever since.

Her hands are always slightly bruised across the knuckles. Percussionist's hands, self-taught, which means she learned the hard way what the correct technique was for.

---

## Personality & Mannerisms

Confrontational by default but not by preference — she's spent three years being dismissed and has stopped softening her entry. When she meets someone who actually listens to what she's saying rather than the way she's saying it, she goes slightly off-balance. She doesn't know quite what to do with people who take her seriously.

She loves Sallowreach. This is important. She is not trying to destroy the Celebration or tear down the tradition. She thinks the tradition has calcified into something that serves the people maintaining it more than the city it belongs to, and she thinks good music should feel like something is at stake every time, and she thinks the Celebration stopped feeling that way about a hundred and fifty years ago and everyone is too polite to say so.

Her argument: *"Who decided the old way was sacred? The people who benefit from everyone else treating it as sacred."*

She will say this to anyone. She has said it to the head of the Musicians' Accord three times. She has not been invited back.

**She talks fast when she's certain about something.** When she's less certain, she gets quieter and more specific. The quiet version is the one worth listening to.

**She performs near the water** when she can't get a venue — sets up on the outer boardwalks with the drum and plays to the Mire. She's noticed the bioluminescence responds differently when she plays her new arrangements versus the traditional ones. She has interpreted this as the Mire preferring her version. This is both wrong and very nearly right.

---

## Background

Self-taught, entirely. She grew up in a family that participated in the Celebration the same way every family in Sallowreach does — fully, sincerely, the old words learned phonetically from a grandmother who learned them phonetically from hers. At twelve she started pulling the drum out between Celebrations and playing with it. At fifteen she was playing for coin on the market boardwalks. At twenty-two she submitted her first formal arrangement to the Musicians' Accord for consideration for an official Celebration slot.

They rejected it. The rhythmic pattern in the second movement deviated from the traditional form. She revised and resubmitted. They rejected it again. After the third rejection she stopped submitting and started playing the arrangement publicly, near the water, where anyone could hear it.

The Accord has since sent two formal letters of concern. She's framed both of them on the wall of the room she rents above a tannery in the Outer Market.

What she doesn't know: that second-movement deviation she couldn't get anyone to reconsider is the specific alteration that disrupts the binding. Her ear found the wrong note in a two-hundred-year-old prison and she's been trying to play it louder ever since.

---

## What She Wants

To perform at the Celebration. Not to replace the tradition — to be part of it, her way. She wants to stand in the middle of sixty thousand people singing the old words and play something that makes the whole thing feel like it means something instead of feeling like everyone is very carefully not changing anything.

She will not compromise the arrangement to get there. She has had this conversation.

---

## What She's Hiding

She's more isolated than she performs. The backlash has been sustained and personal in the way small-city conservatism gets personal, and most of the people she grew up with have quietly stopped coming to her shows to avoid the association. She finds the outer boardwalk audiences — the holdout families, the late-shift workers, the people who don't have Accord memberships to protect — genuinely more sustaining than the venues she's been shut out of, and she's not sure what to do with that.

She's also noticed that when she plays near the water, something responds. The bioluminescence shifts. The pattern changes in a way she hasn't been able to fully document or describe. It excites her and slightly frightens her and she's told exactly no one.

---

## Key Relationships

- [[The Musicians' Accord]] — three formal rejections, two letters of concern; the relationship is adversarial and has been for years
- The outer boardwalk families — her most consistent audience; she knows several by name; she showed up to play the night the first relocation order was posted and nobody asked her to
- [[Caspar Renne]] — has met him twice; he's one of the only people in the city who engaged with her arrangement as a musical and historical question rather than a political one; he told her the old words predate written Sallowreach history, which she found interesting and then filed away; she doesn't know he has the only transcription in existence

## Campaign Function

### Act 1 — Early Encounter
The party finds her playing on the outer boardwalks, or hears about the Accord controversy, or stumbles into a venue where she's performing to a small and slightly guilty-looking crowd. She's easy to like if the party doesn't lead with judgment.

What she demonstrates, without stating it: the Celebration has not changed in two hundred years. Not the words, not the patterns, not the torch positions, not the drum rhythms. When she describes what the Accord told her — *"The rhythmic pattern in the second movement is not the traditional form"* — the party should be able to hear that this is a city that treats a two-century-old ritual as untouchable. Not out of malice. Out of love. That's what makes it hard.

She also shows the party that Sallowreach has its tensions. Not everyone here is in easy agreement about what the city is or should be.

### Act 2-3 — Potential Ally (Option B: Surgical Sabotage)

If the party has built a relationship with her and they come back with the truth — *the Celebration is a two-hundred-year binding ritual; the old words are a binding spell; the drum pattern in the second movement is load-bearing and your arrangement broke it* — she will be very still for a moment.

Then: a slow, incredulous exhale. Not quite a laugh.

*"I've spent three years trying to tell them the old words don't matter. Turns out they matter in exactly the wrong way."*

She's in. She doesn't need convincing, she doesn't need time, she doesn't need the party to justify it. She will want to know what happens to the city when the binding breaks — she's a punk, not a nihilist, and she loves Sallowreach — but if the answer is *the city loses the lumber trade and its primary export and the Celebration changes forever*, she will weigh that for about ten seconds and then ask what she needs to play and when.

She knows the traditional arrangement inside out — you have to, to deviate from it deliberately. She knows which element is load-bearing better than anyone, because she found it by accident. She can perform the disruption as part of a public set, which means sixty thousand people hear the wrong note at the exact right moment and none of them know what she did.

The Accord will know it was her. It will be, technically, the thing that ends her career in Sallowreach.

She will consider this a reasonable trade.

## Dialogue Hooks

**On her arrangement, to anyone who asks:**
*"It's the same song. Same structure, same words, same — I changed the rhythm in one movement. Eight bars. Nobody could tell me WHY those eight bars had to stay that way, just that they did, and that apparently that's enough."*

**On the Accord:**
*"They sent me a letter. Two letters. Very formal. I think they hoped the second one would feel more serious."* [beat] *"I framed them."*

**On the water responding to her music:**
*"You'd have to see it. I've tried to describe it and it sounds like I'm making it up."* [quieter] *"It only does it with my arrangement. Not the traditional. Just mine."*

**If the party tells her the truth about the Celebration:**
*"...How long have you known this?"* [listens to the answer] *"And the Accord has been protecting a prison for two hundred years because nobody translated the words."* [long pause] *"Alright. What do you need me to play?"*

## Stat Block

Not a combat encounter. If somehow cornered, she fights with the drum (improvised weapon, 1d6 bludgeoning) and zero technique and a great deal of commitment. She's been in two actual fights in her life. She won one of them.
